Proving your relationship is real is the single most important part of your K-1 visa petition. It's also where most people fall short.

USCIS processes thousands of K-1 petitions every month, and officers are trained to identify fraudulent relationships. Your job is to make it overwhelmingly obvious that your relationship is genuine — through consistent, varied, and well-organized evidence.

This guide covers every type of evidence USCIS accepts, how to present it effectively, and the common mistakes that lead to Requests for Evidence (RFEs) and denials.

What USCIS Looks For: The "Bona Fide Relationship" Standard

When USCIS adjudicates your I-129F petition, they're answering one central question: Is this a genuine, bona fide relationship with a real intention to marry?

The term "bona fide" is legal Latin for "in good faith." USCIS wants to see that your relationship:

  • Is real and ongoing — not just a one-time meeting
  • Has history and depth — you know each other meaningfully
  • Involves regular communication — you stay connected between visits
  • Includes genuine intent to marry — you've discussed and planned a future together
  • Is recognized by others — friends and family know about the relationship

Officers look at the totality of the evidence. No single piece of evidence makes or breaks your case. It's the overall picture that matters — and the more complete that picture, the better.

Think of your evidence package as telling the story of your relationship. Every piece of evidence is a chapter. The more chapters, and the more detailed they are, the more convincing the story.

The In-Person Meeting Requirement

Before anything else, you must prove that you and your fiancé(e) have physically met in person within the past two years before filing the I-129F. This is a legal requirement under INA Section 214(d).

Evidence to Prove You Met In Person

  • Flight records: Booking confirmations, e-tickets, and boarding passes showing your name, travel dates, and destination
  • Passport stamps: Color copies of entry and exit stamps from both your passport and your fiancé(e)'s passport
  • Hotel or accommodation receipts: Booking confirmations showing dates and location
  • Photos from the visit: Photos together with identifiable landmarks, date stamps, or metadata
  • Receipts and tickets: Restaurant receipts, attraction tickets, transportation receipts from the visit location
  • Visa stamps: If your fiancé(e) needed a visa to visit, include a copy of that visa

What If We Met More Than 2 Years Ago?

You must have met within the 2-year window before filing. If your last meeting was more than 2 years ago, you have two options:

  1. Plan another visit before filing your I-129F
  2. Apply for a waiver if the meeting requirement would cause extreme hardship or violates strict cultural customs (this is difficult to obtain and rarely granted)

What If We Met Online?

Meeting online is fine — millions of couples do. But you must still prove that you've met in person at least once within the past two years. Online-only relationships do not satisfy the meeting requirement, regardless of how long you've been communicating.

Communication Evidence

USCIS wants to see that your relationship involves regular, ongoing communication. This is one of the strongest categories of evidence because it shows the relationship is real and active — not just during visits, but between them.

How to Present Communication Evidence

Don't dump 500 pages of raw chat logs. Instead:

  1. Select representative samples from each month of your relationship
  2. Show variety — include everyday conversations, planning discussions, emotional exchanges, and discussions about the future
  3. Show consistency — demonstrate that communication was regular throughout the relationship, not just in bursts
  4. Highlight key moments — the proposal, first "I love you," meeting each other's families, discussing wedding plans
  5. Include a summary log — a table showing communication frequency by month (e.g., "March 2025: 847 WhatsApp messages, 12 video calls averaging 45 minutes")

How much is enough? Aim for 30-60 pages of communication evidence spanning the full duration of your relationship. A summary log covering every month, plus selected screenshots highlighting key conversations.

Photos: What to Include and How

Photos are the most visually impactful evidence in your petition. A USCIS officer can flip through your photos and immediately get a sense of whether your relationship looks genuine.

Photo Guidelines

  • Quantity: 20-40 photos is the sweet spot. Enough to tell the story without overwhelming.
  • Variety: Include casual and formal, indoor and outdoor, just the two of you and with friends/family
  • Multiple visits: Photos from different trips and time periods show an ongoing relationship
  • Clear and well-printed: Use 4x6 prints on photo paper, or high-quality color printouts
  • Labeled: Every photo should be labeled on the back (or in a caption below) with the date, location, and who's in the photo

Best Types of Photos to Include

  • Together in recognizable public places (landmarks, restaurants, airports)
  • Meeting each other's families and friends
  • Celebrating holidays or birthdays together
  • Everyday moments — cooking, grocery shopping, watching TV
  • Travel photos from multiple visits
  • The proposal (if photographed)
  • Engagement ring photos
  • Wedding planning activities — venue visits, dress shopping, invitation design

Photos to Avoid

  • Overly intimate or inappropriate photos
  • Only selfies with no background context
  • All photos from a single day or visit (this suggests a limited relationship)
  • Blurry or low-quality images where you can't clearly see faces
  • Heavily filtered or edited photos

Financial Evidence of the Relationship

Money talks — and financial evidence can powerfully demonstrate commitment. USCIS looks for financial evidence that shows you've invested real resources into the relationship.

Strong Financial Evidence

  • Flight bookings and receipts: Proof you've spent money traveling to see each other
  • Money transfers: Western Union, Remitly, Wise, or bank transfer receipts showing financial support
  • Joint financial accounts: If you've opened any shared accounts (not required, but very strong)
  • Shared expenses: Receipts from activities during visits — dinners, hotels, excursions
  • Gift purchases and shipping: Receipts for gifts sent to your fiancé(e)
  • Engagement ring receipt: Proof you purchased an engagement ring
  • Wedding deposits or bookings: Venue, caterer, or wedding planner payments
  • Shared subscriptions: Streaming services, family phone plans, etc.

Important: Financial evidence of the relationship is different from the I-134 Affidavit of Support, which proves the petitioner can financially support the fiancé(e). Both are needed, but they serve different purposes.

Affidavits from Friends and Family

Third-party statements add crucial credibility to your petition. When someone outside the relationship vouches for its authenticity, it carries significant weight with USCIS.

What to Include in an Affidavit

Each affidavit should include:

  • Full legal name, address, and contact information of the person writing the statement
  • Their relationship to the couple (friend, parent, sibling, coworker, etc.)
  • How long they've known about the relationship
  • Specific details about witnessing the couple together — visits, phone calls, seeing photos, hearing about plans
  • Their personal belief that the relationship is genuine
  • Signature, date, and notarization (notarization isn't legally required but strongly recommended)
  • Copy of the writer's government-issued ID

Who Should Write Affidavits?

  • Best: Parents or siblings of either partner who have met both people
  • Great: Close friends who have witnessed the relationship firsthand
  • Good: Coworkers, neighbors, or religious leaders who know about the relationship
  • From both sides: Include affidavits from people who know the petitioner AND people who know the beneficiary

How many? Include 2-4 affidavits. More than that adds diminishing returns. Focus on quality and specificity over quantity.

Sample Affidavit Opening

I, [Full Name], residing at [Address], hereby declare under penalty of perjury that the following statements are true and correct to the best of my knowledge. I am the [relationship] of [Petitioner/Beneficiary name]. I have known [Petitioner] for [X years] and have been aware of their relationship with [Beneficiary] since [date]...

Additional Relationship Evidence

Beyond the core categories above, these additional types of evidence can strengthen your petition:

Social Media Evidence

  • Screenshots of relationship status changes on Facebook
  • Public posts about each other, anniversary posts, photos tagged together
  • Comments on each other's posts showing regular interaction
  • Instagram stories or posts from visits together

Travel Planning Documents

  • Itineraries for future trips to see each other
  • Booking confirmations for upcoming visits
  • Travel visa applications showing intent to visit

Wedding Planning Evidence

  • Venue research, tour confirmations, or deposits
  • Save-the-date cards or wedding invitations
  • Wedding planning correspondence (planner emails, vendor quotes)
  • Marriage license applications or research

Evidence of Meeting Each Other's Circles

  • Photos with each other's families
  • Messages in family group chats that include both partners
  • Invitations to family events (holiday dinners, birthdays)
  • Your fiancé(e) being introduced to friends on video calls

Common Mistakes That Trigger RFEs

Avoid these errors when compiling your relationship evidence:

1. Submitting Too Little Evidence

Five photos and two pages of chat logs won't cut it. USCIS officers review hundreds of petitions. Yours needs to stand out with thorough, convincing evidence. If you're wondering "Is this enough?" — it probably isn't. Add more.

2. Evidence from Only One Time Period

If all your evidence is from a single visit or a few months, USCIS will question whether the relationship has depth and longevity. Spread your evidence across the entire timeline of your relationship.

3. No Communication Evidence Between Visits

Photos from two trips a year apart with nothing in between is a red flag. Show that you communicate consistently between visits. Monthly communication samples are ideal.

4. Generic or Vague Affidavits

An affidavit that says "I believe their relationship is real" with no specific details is nearly worthless. Affidavits need concrete details — specific occasions, dates, observations, and personal knowledge.

5. Poorly Organized Submissions

Loose papers, unnumbered pages, unlabeled photos, and random chat screenshots dumped together make it hard for the officer to follow your story. Disorganization doesn't just look unprofessional — it makes your evidence less persuasive.

6. Inconsistent Information

If your petition says you met in June 2024 but your photos are dated April 2024, USCIS will notice. Make sure all dates, locations, and facts are consistent across every piece of evidence.

7. Ignoring Red Flags

Large age gaps, short relationships before engagement, previous visa petitions for other partners, or one partner having a prior visa overstay — these are red flags USCIS watches for. Don't ignore them. Address them head-on with a personal statement and supporting evidence.

How to Organize Your Evidence Package

Good organization can be the difference between approval and an RFE. Here's the system we recommend:

Evidence Binder Structure

TabContents
Tab 1Cover letter and evidence index (list of every document included)
Tab 2Forms (I-129F, G-325A, etc.)
Tab 3In-person meeting evidence (flights, stamps, hotel receipts)
Tab 4Photos (labeled, organized chronologically)
Tab 5Communication evidence (summary log + selected screenshots)
Tab 6Financial evidence (transfers, shared expenses, ring receipt)
Tab 7Affidavits (with copies of IDs)
Tab 8Additional evidence (social media, wedding planning, etc.)
Tab 9Financial support documents (I-134, tax returns, pay stubs)

Formatting Tips

  • Print on standard letter-size paper (8.5 x 11)
  • Use sheet protectors for original documents and photos
  • Number every page and reference page numbers in your cover letter
  • Use color printing for photos, passport stamps, and any image-based evidence
  • Include an evidence index at the front that lists every tab and document
  • Make copies of everything before mailing — keep a complete duplicate for your records
An organized, professional petition tells the officer two things: this person takes the process seriously, and this person has nothing to hide. Both work in your favor.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does USCIS consider a bona fide relationship for the K-1 visa?

A bona fide relationship means you and your fiancé(e) have a genuine, real romantic relationship with a sincere intention to marry. USCIS evaluates this through evidence of ongoing communication, in-person visits, shared financial responsibilities, photos together, affidavits from friends and family, and evidence of wedding planning.

How many photos should I submit with my K-1 visa petition?

Submit 20-40 photos showing you together across multiple visits and time periods. Include a variety — casual moments, meeting family, holidays, travel, and everyday life. Quality and variety matter more than quantity. Each photo should be labeled with the date, location, and who is pictured.

Can WhatsApp and text messages be used as K-1 visa evidence?

Yes. WhatsApp messages, text messages, video call logs, and other digital communication records are accepted as evidence. Print representative samples that show consistent, ongoing communication across the entire relationship. Include screenshots showing dates, both parties' names, and varied conversation topics.

What is an affidavit for a K-1 visa relationship?

An affidavit is a written statement from a friend or family member who can personally attest to the genuineness of your relationship. It should be signed, dated, and ideally notarized. Include 2-4 affidavits from people who have witnessed your relationship firsthand, with specific details and observations.

Do I need to prove we met in person for the K-1 visa?

Yes. The K-1 visa requires that you have physically met your fiancé(e) in person within the past two years before filing. You must provide evidence such as flight records, passport stamps, boarding passes, hotel receipts, and photos from your visit. There are limited exemptions for extreme hardship or cultural customs, but they are rarely granted.

What happens if I don't have enough relationship evidence?

If USCIS determines your relationship evidence is insufficient, they will issue a Request for Evidence (RFE), adding 3-5 months to your processing time. In rare cases with very weak evidence, USCIS may deny the petition outright. It is always better to over-document your relationship from the very beginning.

How far back should my relationship evidence go?

Include evidence spanning the entire length of your relationship — from how you first met to the present. USCIS wants to see the relationship's development over time. Early communication showing how the relationship began, evidence from the middle period showing growth, and recent evidence showing continued strength and wedding planning.

What if we have a large age gap?

An age gap by itself doesn't disqualify you, but USCIS may scrutinize the relationship more closely. Address it proactively: include extra relationship evidence, strong affidavits from people who have seen you together, and a personal statement that addresses the relationship naturally without being defensive.
