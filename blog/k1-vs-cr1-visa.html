K-1 vs CR-1 Visa: Which Should You Choose in 2026?

Last updated: March 2026 · 15 min read

Quick Answer

The K-1 fiance visa gets your partner to the U.S. faster (10-14 months) but costs more ($2,380+) and requires Adjustment of Status after arrival. The CR-1 spouse visa takes longer (12-18 months) but costs less ($1,540 total), and your spouse arrives with a green card and immediate work authorization. Choose K-1 for speed, CR-1 for rights and lower cost.

You've found the person you want to spend your life with. They live in another country. Now you need to figure out the best way to bring them to the United States legally.

You have two main options: the K-1 fiance visa and the CR-1 spouse visa. Both get your partner to the U.S. Both lead to a green card. But the path each one takes is dramatically different, and picking the wrong one can cost you thousands of dollars and months of unnecessary waiting.

This guide breaks down the K-1 vs CR-1 visa comparison honestly. No sugarcoating, no pushing you toward one over the other. Just the facts, the numbers, and the real-world trade-offs so you can make the right decision for your specific situation in 2026.

K-1 vs CR-1: The Quick Overview

Here's the fundamental difference between the fiance visa and the spouse visa:

  • K-1 (Fiance Visa): You are NOT married yet. Your fiance enters the U.S. on a temporary visa, you marry within 90 days, and then they apply for a green card through Adjustment of Status (AOS).
  • CR-1 (Spouse Visa): You ARE already married. Your spouse goes through consular processing abroad, and when they enter the U.S., they arrive as a lawful permanent resident with a green card already in hand.

That single distinction — married vs. not yet married — shapes everything else: the cost, the timeline, the paperwork, the ability to work, and the ability to travel.

The K-1 visa gets your partner here faster. The CR-1 visa gets them here with more rights. That's the core trade-off.

How the K-1 Fiance Visa Works

The K-1 visa is a non-immigrant visa that allows the foreign fiance(e) of a U.S. citizen to enter the United States for the purpose of getting married. Here's the step-by-step process:

  1. U.S. citizen files Form I-129F (Petition for Alien Fiance) with USCIS. Filing fee: $675.
  2. USCIS reviews and approves the petition. Current processing time: 5-9 months.
  3. Case transfers to the National Visa Center (NVC), which forwards it to the appropriate U.S. embassy or consulate abroad.
  4. Foreign fiance completes Form DS-160, gathers documents, gets a medical exam, and attends an interview at the embassy. Visa fee: $265.
  5. If approved, the fiance receives the K-1 visa and has 6 months to enter the U.S.
  6. After entering the U.S., the couple must marry within 90 days.
  7. After marriage, the foreign spouse files for Adjustment of Status (AOS) using Form I-485 to get a green card. Filing fee: $1,440.
  8. While AOS is pending, the spouse can apply for an Employment Authorization Document (EAD) and Advance Parole (travel permission). These are included in the $1,440 fee but take 3-5 months to arrive.

Total process time from filing to green card: approximately 10-14 months to enter the U.S., plus 8-14 months for AOS approval after that. For a deeper dive on timing, see our K-1 visa processing time breakdown.

How the CR-1 Spouse Visa Works

The CR-1 visa is an immigrant visa for the foreign spouse of a U.S. citizen. The key requirement: you must already be legally married. Here's the process:

  1. U.S. citizen files Form I-130 (Petition for Alien Relative) with USCIS. Filing fee: $625.
  2. USCIS reviews and approves the petition. Current processing time: 5-11 months.
  3. Case transfers to the NVC. NVC collects fees ($325 immigrant visa fee), the DS-260 immigrant visa application, financial documents (Affidavit of Support), and civil documents.
  4. Foreign spouse gets a medical exam and attends an interview at the U.S. embassy or consulate.
  5. If approved, the spouse receives an immigrant visa and has 6 months to enter the U.S.
  6. Upon entry, the spouse is a lawful permanent resident. Their green card arrives by mail within weeks. There is a $220 USCIS immigrant fee paid before or after entry.
  7. No Adjustment of Status needed. No separate work permit application. No Advance Parole needed for travel.

Total process time from filing to U.S. entry: approximately 12-18 months. But once they arrive, they are done — green card in hand, authorized to work, free to travel.

The Real Cost Difference

The CR-1 visa saves you approximately $840-$1,090 in government fees compared to the K-1. The reason is simple: the K-1 requires a separate Adjustment of Status filing after marriage, which costs $1,440 on its own. The CR-1 bypasses that entirely because your spouse arrives as a permanent resident.

When you add in additional costs like medical exams ($200-$500 for either), document fees ($100-$500), and professional services ($500-$5,000), the total out-of-pocket difference narrows slightly. But the CR-1 remains cheaper in nearly every case.

However — and this is important — the CR-1 requires you to get married abroad first. That means a trip to your partner's country (or a third country), which can cost $1,000-$5,000+ in flights, lodging, and local marriage fees. If you haven't already visited recently and don't have a wedding planned, this travel cost can offset some of the government fee savings.

Time to Green Card

This is where the CR-1 pulls ahead dramatically:

  • K-1: After entering the U.S. on the K-1 visa, you must marry within 90 days, then file AOS. AOS processing currently takes 8-14 months. Total time from initial filing to green card: 18-28 months.
  • CR-1: Your spouse arrives WITH the green card. Total time from initial filing to green card: 12-18 months.

So while the K-1 gets your partner to the U.S. sooner, the CR-1 gets them a green card sooner. The K-1 entrant then faces months of waiting for AOS approval, during which they have limited work and travel rights.

Work Authorization Comparison

This is one of the most significant practical differences between the K-1 and CR-1, and it's often underestimated by couples making their decision.

K-1 Visa: Cannot Work Immediately

When your fiance enters the U.S. on a K-1 visa, they cannot legally work. Here's the timeline:

  1. Enter the U.S. on K-1 visa — no work authorization.
  2. Get married within 90 days — still no work authorization.
  3. File AOS (I-485) with an EAD application (I-765) — still no work authorization.
  4. Wait 3-5 months for the EAD to arrive — finally authorized to work.

That's potentially 4-8 months after arriving in the U.S. with no ability to earn income. For many couples, this creates real financial strain. Your partner can't contribute to bills, can't start building a career, and can't even take a part-time job to stay busy. It's one of the biggest pain points of the K-1 process.

CR-1 Visa: Can Work Immediately

A CR-1 spouse enters the U.S. as a lawful permanent resident. They are authorized to work from day one. Their green card (the physical card) arrives by mail within a few weeks, but they have legal work authorization the moment they step through immigration at the airport.

They can apply for a Social Security number, start job hunting, and begin working with no additional applications or waiting periods.

If your partner needs to work soon after arrival — whether to contribute financially, to maintain their career momentum, or simply for their mental well-being — the CR-1 is the clear winner on work authorization.

Travel Ability Comparison

Another critical difference that affects daily life after arrival.

K-1 Visa: Stuck in the U.S. for Months

After entering on a K-1 visa, your fiance should not leave the United States until they have an approved Advance Parole document. Leaving without Advance Parole while AOS is pending is considered abandoning the application — they could be barred from re-entering.

Advance Parole is filed along with the AOS package and takes 3-5 months to arrive. Until then, no international travel. No visiting family back home. No international business trips. No emergencies abroad.

This can be emotionally difficult, especially if your partner has close family or ongoing commitments in their home country.

CR-1 Visa: Free to Travel from Day One

CR-1 entrants have a green card. They can travel internationally whenever they want (subject to normal green card holder rules about not abandoning residency). No extra applications, no waiting periods, no restrictions beyond common sense.

Want to visit their family a month after arriving? No problem. Need to fly abroad for work? Go ahead. Have a family emergency? They can be on a plane that day.

Choose the K-1 Visa If...

The K-1 fiance visa is the better choice when the following conditions apply to your situation:

  • You are not yet married and want your wedding in the U.S. Many couples want their U.S.-based family and friends at the wedding. The K-1 makes this possible since the marriage happens after arrival.
  • Being together quickly is your top priority. The K-1 typically gets your partner to the U.S. 2-6 months faster than the CR-1. If the separation is unbearable and every month matters, the K-1 wins on speed to entry.
  • Getting married abroad is logistically difficult or impossible. Some countries make it extremely complicated for foreigners to marry there. Others have long bureaucratic processes. If marrying abroad would require months of additional effort, the K-1 avoids that entirely.
  • Your partner has children who need to come too. K-2 derivative visas for children under 21 are processed alongside the K-1, which can simplify things for families.
  • Work authorization timing is not a concern. If your household can comfortably live on one income for 4-8 months while your partner waits for their EAD, the work authorization delay won't be a serious issue.
  • Your partner has no immediate need to travel internationally. If they won't need to leave the U.S. during the AOS waiting period, the travel restrictions won't matter.

Choose the CR-1 Visa If...

The CR-1 spouse visa is the better choice when these conditions apply:

  • You are already married or can easily marry abroad. If you've already tied the knot, the CR-1 is almost always the better path. If you're planning a trip to your partner's country anyway, getting legally married during that visit opens up the CR-1 option.
  • You want to save money. The CR-1 saves $840+ in government fees by eliminating the Adjustment of Status step. Over the full process, that's real money.
  • Your partner needs to work as soon as possible. No 3-5 month EAD wait. No financial stress from months of unemployment. Your spouse can work from the day they arrive.
  • Your partner needs to travel internationally after arrival. Close family abroad, international work obligations, or simply the peace of mind that they can leave and come back freely — the CR-1 provides this immediately.
  • You want less paperwork overall. The CR-1 is a single process. File I-130, go through consular processing, arrive with a green card. No second major filing (AOS), no EAD application, no Advance Parole application. One and done.
  • You want a green card sooner. Despite the slightly longer wait to enter the U.S., the CR-1 delivers a green card faster overall because there's no AOS queue after arrival.
  • You can wait a couple extra months to be together. If the 2-6 month difference in U.S. entry time is manageable, the CR-1's benefits almost always outweigh the K-1's speed advantage.

The Honest Truth About K-1 vs CR-1

Here's what most websites won't tell you: the CR-1 is the objectively better visa in most situations. It's cheaper, it results in a green card faster, it gives your spouse more rights upon arrival, and it involves less total paperwork.

So why does the K-1 exist? And why do so many couples still choose it?

The K-1 Exists for a Real Reason

The K-1 was created for couples who want to get married in the United States. It was designed for the scenario where two people are engaged and want their wedding on American soil, surrounded by their American friends and family. That's a perfectly valid reason to choose it.

It's also the right choice when the timing difference truly matters. If you've been separated for two years and every additional month apart is agonizing, the K-1's faster entry time is worth the extra cost and complexity.

When the K-1 Is Actually the Wrong Choice

The K-1 is the wrong choice when couples pick it simply because they haven't gotten married yet and assume the K-1 is the "first step." Many couples don't realize that flying to their partner's country, getting married there (even a simple legal ceremony), and then filing the CR-1 would save them money, get a green card faster, and avoid the AOS headache entirely.

We've seen countless couples who chose the K-1 and then said: "If I'd known about the work permit wait, the travel restrictions, and the extra cost, I would have just gotten married on my next visit and filed the CR-1."

The Hybrid Strategy

Some smart couples use a hybrid approach:

  1. Get legally married abroad in a simple civil ceremony (just the legal paperwork, no big celebration).
  2. File the I-130 for the CR-1 visa.
  3. Plan a "real" wedding celebration in the U.S. after the spouse arrives with their green card.

This gives you the best of both worlds: the CR-1's lower cost, faster green card, and immediate work/travel rights, PLUS a U.S. wedding celebration with everyone you love. The legal marriage abroad is just the paperwork — the party happens here.

What About Process Complexity?

The K-1 process is more complex because it's essentially two processes bolted together: the K-1 visa to enter, then the full Adjustment of Status to get the green card. Each of those stages involves its own forms, fees, evidence requirements, biometrics appointments, and waiting periods.

The CR-1 is a single continuous process. You file I-130, go through NVC, do the embassy interview, and your spouse arrives as a permanent resident. One filing fee, one interview, one wait, done.

For couples who find immigration paperwork stressful (which is most couples), the CR-1's simpler process is a significant quality-of-life advantage.

The Bottom Line

If you're already married or can easily marry abroad: choose the CR-1. It's cheaper, simpler, and gives your spouse more rights upon arrival.

If you're not married and specifically want to marry in the U.S., or if the timing difference between the K-1 and CR-1 is critical to your relationship: choose the K-1. Just go in with eyes open about the costs, the EAD wait, and the travel restrictions.

Either way, the most important thing is filing correctly the first time. A Request for Evidence (RFE) or a denial costs you months of additional waiting — and sometimes the entire filing fee. Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between the K-1 and CR-1 visa?

The K-1 (fiance visa) allows your unmarried partner to enter the U.S. and you marry within 90 days, then apply for a green card through Adjustment of Status. The CR-1 (spouse visa) requires you to marry abroad first, and your spouse enters the U.S. as a lawful permanent resident with a green card already approved. The CR-1 is cheaper ($1,540 vs $2,380 in government fees) and results in a green card sooner.

Which is cheaper, K-1 or CR-1?

The CR-1 is cheaper. CR-1 government fees total approximately $1,540 compared to approximately $2,380 for the K-1 visa. The K-1 costs more because it requires a separate Adjustment of Status filing ($1,440) after marriage. However, the CR-1 may require travel costs for marrying abroad, which should be factored into your total budget. For a complete K-1 cost breakdown, see our K-1 visa cost guide.

Which is faster, K-1 or CR-1?

It depends on what you mean by "faster." The K-1 gets your partner into the U.S. sooner (10-14 months vs 12-18 months). But the CR-1 gets them a green card sooner because the K-1 entrant must then wait 8-14 months for Adjustment of Status approval. Total time to green card: K-1 is 18-28 months, CR-1 is 12-18 months.

Can I work on a K-1 visa?

Not immediately. After entering on a K-1 visa and marrying, you must file for Adjustment of Status and apply for an Employment Authorization Document (EAD). The EAD currently takes 3-5 months to arrive. During that time, you cannot legally work. CR-1 entrants, by contrast, can work from day one.

Can I switch from K-1 to CR-1 or vice versa?

You can switch strategies, but it means starting over. If you've filed I-129F (K-1) and then get married abroad, you would withdraw the K-1 petition and file I-130 (CR-1) instead. You cannot convert one petition into the other. Any fees paid are non-refundable.

Can I travel outside the U.S. on a K-1 visa?

After entering on the K-1 and filing for AOS, you should NOT travel internationally until you receive an approved Advance Parole document. Leaving without it could be considered abandoning your AOS application. Advance Parole takes 3-5 months to arrive. CR-1 entrants face no such restriction.

Do I need a lawyer for the K-1 or CR-1 visa?

A lawyer is not required for either visa. Many couples successfully file on their own or with professional document preparation assistance. However, if your case involves complications like prior visa denials, criminal history, prior immigration violations, or previous marriages, consulting an attorney is recommended. Our document preparation service includes attorney review for complex situations.

What if we get married while the K-1 petition is pending?

If you marry while a K-1 petition (I-129F) is pending, the K-1 petition is automatically invalid — the K-1 is only for unmarried couples. You would need to withdraw the I-129F and file an I-130 for the CR-1 instead. The I-129F filing fee ($675) is non-refundable.

Does the K-1 or CR-1 lead to citizenship faster?

The path to citizenship takes the same total time for both visas. Once your spouse has their green card (whether through K-1 + AOS or CR-1), they can apply for U.S. citizenship after 3 years of permanent residence while married to a U.S. citizen. The CR-1 may get them to citizenship slightly sooner simply because the green card arrives sooner.
