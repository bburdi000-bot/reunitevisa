Colombia is one of the top countries for K-1 fiancé visa petitions filed from the United States. Whether your fiancé(e) is from Bogotá, Medellín, Cali, Barranquilla, or anywhere else in Colombia, the K-1 visa process follows the same path — and it all runs through the U.S. Embassy in Bogotá.

This guide covers every step of the K-1 visa process specific to Colombia in 2026: the documents your fiancé(e) needs, how to navigate the Bogotá embassy, what the IOM medical exam involves, exactly what to expect at the interview, and realistic timelines and costs.

K-1 Visa from Colombia: The Big Picture

The K-1 fiancé visa allows a U.S. citizen to bring their foreign fiancé(e) to the United States for the purpose of getting married within 90 days of arrival. For Colombian nationals, the process involves three main stages:

  1. USCIS petition (U.S. side): The U.S. citizen files Form I-129F with USCIS
  2. National Visa Center (NVC): After approval, the case transfers to NVC for processing and forwarding to the embassy
  3. Bogotá embassy (Colombia side): Your fiancé(e) completes the medical exam, gathers Colombian documents, attends the interview, and receives the visa

Good news for Colombia cases: The U.S. Embassy in Bogotá is one of the higher-volume and more efficient embassies for K-1 visa processing. Interview wait times are generally shorter than many other countries, and the embassy staff is experienced with fiancé visa cases.

Step-by-Step Process

Step 1: File Form I-129F with USCIS

The U.S. citizen petitioner files the I-129F petition from the United States. This includes the completed form, filing fee ($675), relationship evidence, proof of the in-person meeting, proof of U.S. citizenship, and the I-134 Affidavit of Support.

Current processing time: 8-12 months at the USCIS California or Texas Service Center.

Step 2: USCIS Approval and NVC Transfer

Once USCIS approves the I-129F, the case is automatically transferred to the National Visa Center (NVC). NVC assigns a case number, sends instructions to the beneficiary, and forwards the case to the U.S. Embassy in Bogotá.

NVC processing time: 4-8 weeks.

Step 3: Embassy Receives the Case

The Bogotá embassy receives the case from NVC and sends your fiancé(e) an instruction packet (often called "Packet 3" or the DS-160 instructions) with details on how to schedule the interview, complete forms, and gather required documents.

Step 4: Your Fiancé(e) Prepares in Colombia

While waiting for the interview appointment, your fiancé(e) needs to:

  • Complete the DS-160 online visa application
  • Pay the $265 visa application fee
  • Schedule and complete the medical exam at the IOM clinic
  • Gather all required Colombian documents
  • Prepare for the interview

Step 5: Attend the Interview in Bogotá

Your fiancé(e) attends the visa interview at the U.S. Embassy. If approved, they receive their visa within 1-2 weeks and can travel to the United States.

Step 6: Enter the U.S. and Marry Within 90 Days

After arrival, you must get married within 90 days. Then file for Adjustment of Status (I-485) to obtain a green card.

The U.S. Embassy in Bogotá handles all K-1 visa interviews for Colombia. Here are the details your fiancé(e) needs:

Getting to the Embassy

If your fiancé(e) lives outside Bogotá, they'll need to travel there for the interview. Plan to arrive the day before — morning interviews mean early wake-ups, and Bogotá traffic is unpredictable. Budget for a hotel near the embassy and local transportation.

From Medellín: 1-hour flight or 8-hour bus ride. From Cali: 1-hour flight or 10-hour bus ride. From Barranquilla: 1.5-hour flight or 16-hour bus ride.

Colombian Documents You Need

Your fiancé(e) must bring the following documents to the Bogotá interview. Start gathering these as soon as the case transfers to NVC — some take weeks to obtain.

Required Documents Checklist

  • Valid Colombian passport — must be valid for at least 6 months beyond the intended entry date
  • DS-160 confirmation page — printed after completing the online application
  • Interview appointment letter
  • Two passport-style photos — 2x2 inches, white background, taken within the past 6 months
  • Birth certificate (Registro Civil de Nacimiento) — certified copy from the Registraduría Nacional
  • Judicial background certificate (Certificado de Antecedentes Judiciales) — from the Policía Nacional
  • Military service booklet (Libreta Militar) — required for Colombian males (ages 18-50)
  • Divorce decree or death certificate — if either party was previously married
  • Medical exam results — sealed envelope from the IOM clinic
  • Evidence of relationship — photos, communication records, affidavits
  • Evidence of financial support — I-134, tax returns, pay stubs from the U.S. petitioner
  • Proof of in-person meeting — flight records, passport stamps, photos

Judicial Background Certificate (Certificado de Antecedentes Judiciales)

This is Colombia's police clearance certificate. It's required by the U.S. Embassy and confirms whether your fiancé(e) has a criminal record in Colombia.

How to Obtain It

  1. Go to policia.gov.co
  2. Navigate to "Antecedentes" and then "Consulta de Antecedentes"
  3. Enter your fiancé(e)'s cédula (Colombian national ID) number
  4. The certificate generates immediately and can be printed

Cost: Free.

Validity: The certificate should be recent — obtain it within 3 months of the interview date.

Important: If your fiancé(e) has lived in any other country for more than 6 months since age 16, they need a police clearance from that country as well.

What If There's a Criminal Record?

A criminal record doesn't automatically disqualify your fiancé(e), but it will require additional documentation. You'll need certified court records, a personal statement explaining the circumstances, and potentially a waiver (Form I-212 or I-601) depending on the offense. Consult with an immigration attorney if this applies to your case.

Civil Registry (Registro Civil)

The Registro Civil de Nacimiento is the official Colombian birth certificate. It's one of the most important documents for the K-1 visa process.

How to Obtain It

  1. Visit any Registraduría Nacional del Estado Civil office in Colombia
  2. Request a certified copy ("copia auténtica") of the Registro Civil de Nacimiento
  3. Your fiancé(e) must bring their cédula de ciudadanía
  4. The office will issue a certified copy, usually within the same day or a few days

Cost: Approximately 10,000-20,000 COP (roughly $2-$5 USD).

Translation: The birth certificate must be accompanied by a certified English translation. Use a professional translation service — the translator must certify their competence and the accuracy of the translation.

If Previously Married

If your fiancé(e) was previously married in Colombia, they need:

  • Divorce decree: Certified copy of the sentencia de divorcio from the court that issued it, or registration of the divorce on the civil registry
  • Updated Registro Civil: The birth certificate should show the annotation (nota marginal) reflecting the divorce
  • If widowed: Death certificate of the former spouse

All of these documents need certified English translations.

Medical Exam at the IOM Clinic

The medical examination for the K-1 visa in Colombia is conducted exclusively by the International Organization for Migration (IOM). The U.S. Embassy does not accept medical exams from any other provider.

IOM Clinic Details

DetailInformation
LocationBogotá (main clinic). Check IOM Colombia website for current address.
AppointmentRequired — schedule in advance through IOM Colombia website or by phone
Cost$150-$200 USD (varies based on vaccinations needed)
PaymentCash or credit card (confirm current payment methods when scheduling)
Duration2-4 hours for the initial visit; may require a follow-up
Results ready3-5 business days (provided in a sealed envelope)

What the Medical Exam Includes

  • Physical examination — general health assessment by a panel physician
  • Blood tests — screening for syphilis and other conditions
  • Chest X-ray — tuberculosis screening
  • Vaccination review — the doctor checks your fiancé(e)'s vaccination history against U.S. requirements
  • Required vaccinations — any missing vaccinations from the CDC-required list must be administered (additional cost)

What to Bring to the Medical Exam

  • Valid passport
  • Cédula de ciudadanía
  • Vaccination records (carné de vacunación) — bring everything you have
  • Two passport-style photos
  • Appointment confirmation
  • Payment

Vaccination Requirements

The CDC requires the following vaccinations for immigrant visa applicants. Your fiancé(e) should ensure these are up to date before the IOM exam to avoid delays and extra costs:

  • MMR (measles, mumps, rubella)
  • Hepatitis A and B
  • Tetanus and diphtheria (Td/Tdap)
  • Varicella (chickenpox)
  • Influenza (seasonal)
  • COVID-19 (check current requirements)
  • Polio, meningococcal, and others depending on age

Tip: If your fiancé(e) can locate childhood vaccination records, bring them. Missing records mean the IOM may need to re-administer vaccinations, which adds $100-$300 to the medical exam cost.

Schedule the medical exam at least 2-3 weeks before the interview date. Results take 3-5 business days, and you don't want a delay in results to force you to reschedule your embassy interview.

Interview Tips Specific to Bogotá

The K-1 visa interview at the Bogotá embassy typically lasts 10-20 minutes. Here's what your fiancé(e) should know:

Common Interview Questions

  • How did you and your fiancé(e) meet?
  • How long have you been in a relationship?
  • When and where did you last see each other?
  • How often do you communicate? By what method?
  • Tell me about your fiancé(e). What do they do for work? Where do they live?
  • When and where do you plan to get married?
  • Have you met each other's families?
  • What are your plans after you arrive in the United States?
  • Has either of you been married before?

Interview Tips

  1. Be honest. Inconsistencies between the interview and the petition are the number one reason for denial. If the officer catches a lie, the case is over.
  2. Know the details. Your fiancé(e) should know your full name, birthday, address, employer, family members' names, and your relationship timeline cold.
  3. Be specific. "We talk every day on WhatsApp and video call 3-4 times a week" is better than "We talk a lot."
  4. Bring organized documents. Have everything in a labeled folder with tabs. When the officer asks for a document, your fiancé(e) should be able to find it immediately.
  5. Dress professionally. Business casual. This is a formal government interview — first impressions matter.
  6. Stay calm. Officers at the Bogotá embassy are generally professional and straightforward. Nervousness is normal and understood — just answer clearly and honestly.
  7. Bring extra relationship evidence. Even if not specifically requested, bringing additional photos, recent communication logs, and proof of continued contact can help if the officer has questions.

After the Interview

There are three possible outcomes:

  • Approved: The officer tells your fiancé(e) the visa is approved. They'll receive instructions to pick up the passport with the visa stamp, typically within 1-2 weeks. They may also use a courier service to return the passport.
  • Administrative processing (221(g)): The case needs additional review. This can take weeks to months. The officer will provide a colored slip explaining what's needed.
  • Denied: Rare in cases with good evidence, but possible if the officer determines the relationship isn't genuine or there's a disqualifying factor.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does the K-1 visa process take from Colombia in 2026?

The total process takes approximately 12-16 months. The longest stage is USCIS I-129F processing (8-12 months). NVC processing takes 4-8 weeks, followed by 2-4 weeks for the Bogotá embassy stage including the medical exam and interview.

Where is the K-1 visa interview conducted in Colombia?

All K-1 visa interviews are conducted at the U.S. Embassy in Bogotá, located at Calle 24 Bis No. 48-50. Even if your fiancé(e) lives in Medellín, Cali, Barranquilla, or anywhere else in Colombia, they must travel to Bogotá for the interview.

What is the judicial background certificate?

The certificado de antecedentes judiciales is Colombia's police clearance certificate, issued by the Policía Nacional. It confirms whether the applicant has a criminal record. It can be obtained free of charge online at policia.gov.co using the cédula number.

Where do I get the K-1 visa medical exam in Colombia?

The medical exam must be completed at an IOM (International Organization for Migration) clinic in Bogotá. No other clinic is accepted. Schedule the appointment in advance through the IOM Colombia website. The exam costs $150-$200, with additional costs for required vaccinations.

How much does the K-1 visa cost from Colombia?

Total costs range from $2,500-$5,000. Government fees total $2,380 (I-129F: $675, DS-160: $265, AOS: $1,440). Additional costs include the medical exam ($150-$200), document translations ($50-$200), travel to Bogotá, and the flight to the United States.

Does my Colombian fiancé(e) need documents apostilled?

The Bogotá embassy generally accepts certified copies from the Registraduría Nacional without a separate apostille. However, requirements can change, so confirm current requirements when your case reaches the embassy stage. All non-English documents need certified translations.

Can the interview be done in Spanish?

Yes. Consular officers at the Bogotá embassy typically speak Spanish, and interviews can be conducted in Spanish. If needed, interpretation services are available. Your fiancé(e) does not need to speak English for the K-1 visa interview.

What if my fiancé(e) has a Colombian military service issue?

Colombian males are required to have a libreta militar (military service booklet). If your fiancé(e) hasn't resolved their military service obligation, it could complicate the process. Resolve any outstanding military service issues before the interview. The libreta militar should be brought to the embassy interview.
