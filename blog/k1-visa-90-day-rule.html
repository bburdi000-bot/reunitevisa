Your K-1 visa is approved. Your fiancé is on a plane to the United States. After months (or years) of waiting, you're finally going to be together. Congratulations — seriously.

But the K-1 process isn't over yet. In fact, one of the most critical phases is just beginning: the 90-day window.

From the moment your fiancé steps through U.S. customs, a clock starts ticking. You have exactly 90 days to get legally married. Not engaged. Not "planning to." Married. And after that, you need to file for your spouse's green card immediately.

This guide covers everything that happens after your fiancé arrives — the marriage deadline, wedding logistics, and every post-marriage step to get your spouse on the path to permanent residency.

What Is the 90-Day Rule?

The K-1 visa (fiance visa) admits your fiancé to the United States for one purpose: to marry the U.S. citizen who petitioned for them within 90 days of arrival.

This isn't a suggestion. It's the legal condition of entry. When your fiancé enters the U.S. on a K-1 visa, they are admitted as a nonimmigrant with the explicit understanding that:

  1. They will marry the petitioner (you) within 90 days
  2. After marriage, they will file for Adjustment of Status (green card)
  3. If they do not marry within 90 days, they must leave the country

There are no extensions. No exceptions. No waivers. The 90-day period is absolute.

Think of the K-1 visa as a 90-day admission to get married and start the green card process. Everything else — the big wedding, the honeymoon, settling in — fits around this legal framework.

When the Clock Starts (And What Counts)

Day 1: The Date of Physical Entry

The 90 days begin on the date your fiancé physically enters the United States and clears customs. This is stamped in their passport by Customs and Border Protection (CBP). The stamp will typically say "K-1" and include the date of entry and the date the 90-day period expires.

Important: The date that matters is the date of entry, not the date the visa was issued or the date of the embassy interview.

Day 90: What Needs to Happen By Then

By day 90, you must have a legally valid marriage. This means:

  • A marriage license issued by a U.S. state or territory
  • A ceremony performed by someone legally authorized to officiate
  • A signed marriage certificate filed with the local clerk

The marriage must be to the same person who filed the K-1 petition. You cannot marry someone else on a K-1 visa.

What Counts as a Valid Marriage

  • Courthouse civil ceremony — yes
  • Church or religious ceremony (with valid license) — yes
  • Backyard wedding with a licensed officiant — yes
  • Las Vegas chapel wedding — yes
  • Online ceremony / proxy marriage — generally no (varies by state)
  • Common-law marriage — only in states that recognize it

The simplest approach: get a marriage license from your county clerk's office, have a civil ceremony, and you're done. You can always have a bigger celebration later.

What Happens If You Don't Marry in Time

Let's address the worst-case scenario, because it's important to understand the stakes.

If the 90 Days Pass Without Marriage:

  • Your fiancé's legal status expires. On day 91, they are unlawfully present in the United States.
  • They must leave the country. Voluntarily departing is better than being removed, but either way, they need to go.
  • Unlawful presence triggers bars. Staying past 90 days begins accumulating unlawful presence, which can trigger a 3-year bar (for 180 days to 1 year of unlawful presence) or a 10-year bar (for more than 1 year) on future U.S. entry.
  • You can refile. If you still want to bring your fiancé to the U.S., you can file a new I-129F petition. But you'll pay the $675 fee again and restart the 6-10 month wait from scratch.

Can You Get an Extension?

No. There is no form, no fee, no waiver, and no emergency request that can extend the 90-day period. Not even if there's a medical emergency, a natural disaster, or a family crisis. The law is absolute on this point.

What If You Decide Not to Get Married?

It happens. Sometimes couples realize after the fiancé arrives that the relationship isn't going to work. In that case:

  • Your fiancé should leave the U.S. before the 90 days expire
  • They are not penalized for using the K-1 visa and then leaving (as long as they leave on time)
  • They can apply for other visas in the future without a black mark
  • Marrying solely to maintain immigration status — when you don't intend to have a real marriage — is immigration fraud and carries severe criminal penalties
Never marry just to "not waste the visa." If the relationship isn't right, it's far better to leave and refile later than to commit fraud.

Wedding Planning on a 90-Day Deadline

90 days sounds like plenty of time. In practice, it goes fast — especially when you're also dealing with your fiancé adjusting to a new country, potential jet lag, meeting family, and all the logistical details of combining two lives.

The Smart Timeline

WhenWhat to Do
Before arrivalResearch marriage license requirements in your state. Some states have waiting periods (3-7 days between license and ceremony).
Week 1Get the marriage license. Bring your fiancé's passport, K-1 visa, I-94 record, birth certificate, and any divorce decrees.
Weeks 2-4Hold the ceremony. Many couples do a simple courthouse wedding first, then plan a larger celebration later.
Weeks 3-6Gather AOS documents: marriage certificate, photos from wedding, joint finances (if any), I-485 forms.
Weeks 4-8File Adjustment of Status (I-485, I-765, I-131). The sooner the better.

Tip: Get the Legal Marriage Done Early

The most stress-free approach is to get legally married within the first 2-4 weeks. A courthouse ceremony takes 15 minutes and costs $30-$100 for the license and fee. Once you're legally married, the 90-day pressure is off, and you can plan a bigger celebration on your own timeline — 6 months, a year, whenever you want.

State Waiting Periods to Watch

Some states impose a waiting period between getting the marriage license and having the ceremony:

  • No waiting period: California, Nevada, Texas, Florida, New York, and most states
  • 1-day wait: Delaware, South Carolina
  • 3-day wait: Alaska, Iowa, Kansas, Massachusetts, Michigan, Montana, Oregon, Washington
  • 5-day wait: Minnesota
  • 6-day wait: Wisconsin

Check your specific county's requirements. Some states waive the waiting period for certain circumstances or if you attend a marriage preparation course.

Getting a Marriage License as a K-1 Couple

Your fiancé is a foreign national with limited U.S. documentation. Here's what you'll typically need at the county clerk's office:

Documents for the Foreign Fiancé

  • Valid passport with K-1 visa stamp
  • I-94 arrival/departure record (print from CBP website: i94.cbp.dhs.gov)
  • Birth certificate with certified English translation
  • Divorce decree or death certificate (if previously married) with translation

Documents for the U.S. Citizen

  • Government-issued ID (driver's license, passport)
  • Social Security number
  • Divorce decree (if previously married)

Common Issues

  • No Social Security number for your fiancé: Most states don't require one. If asked, explain they just arrived on a K-1 visa.
  • Clerk doesn't recognize the K-1 visa: Some small-town clerks have never seen one. Bring a printout of the relevant USCIS page explaining K-1 status. Be patient.
  • Name discrepancies: If your fiancé's passport name doesn't match their birth certificate exactly, bring both documents and explain the naming convention from their country.

After the Wedding: Adjustment of Status

Getting married is the halfway point, not the finish line. After the wedding, your new spouse needs to apply for a green card through Adjustment of Status (AOS).

What to File

The AOS package includes three forms, all filed together with a single $1,440 fee:

FormPurpose
I-485Application to Register Permanent Residence (the green card application)
I-765Application for Employment Authorization Document (work permit)
I-131Application for Travel Document (Advance Parole)
I-864Affidavit of Support (proves you can financially support your spouse)

Documents to Include

  • Certified copy of your marriage certificate
  • Photos from your wedding
  • Copy of your spouse's passport, K-1 visa, and I-94
  • Copy of the I-129F approval notice
  • Birth certificates for both spouses
  • Your tax returns, W-2s, and pay stubs (for I-864)
  • Evidence of bona fide marriage (joint accounts, lease, insurance, etc.)
  • 2 passport-style photos of your spouse
  • Medical exam results (if not done during K-1 process or if expired)

When to File

File as soon as possible after the wedding — ideally within 1-2 weeks. While there's no hard deadline (other than that your spouse should maintain continuous legal status), prompt filing:

  • Starts the clock on the work permit (EAD) sooner
  • Starts the clock on travel authorization sooner
  • Demonstrates to USCIS that you're following the process in good faith

Total filing fee: $1,440 — covers the I-485, I-765, and I-131. See our complete K-1 visa cost breakdown for all fees.

Work Permit and Travel Authorization

Employment Authorization Document (EAD)

Your spouse cannot legally work in the U.S. until they receive the EAD card. Current processing times:

  • 3-6 months after filing I-765 (filed with AOS)
  • The EAD is a physical card mailed to your address
  • Once received, your spouse can work for any employer
  • Your spouse will also receive a Social Security number (if applied for on the I-765)

Financial planning tip: Plan for your household to live on one income for 3-6 months after the wedding. This is a real financial strain for many couples. Budget for it before your fiancé arrives.

Advance Parole (Travel Document)

After filing AOS, your spouse cannot leave the United States until they receive Advance Parole approval. If they leave without it, USCIS considers the AOS application abandoned — meaning they lose their green card application and their legal status.

  • Processing time: 3-6 months (often arrives around the same time as the EAD)
  • Combo card: USCIS now issues a combo EAD/AP card, so one card covers both work and travel authorization
  • Important: Even with Advance Parole, extended trips abroad can raise questions about intent to reside in the U.S.

Conditional Green Card: What to Expect

Because you married within 2 years of your spouse receiving immigrant status, your spouse will receive a conditional green card (valid for 2 years, not 10).

The AOS Interview

USCIS will schedule an in-person interview at your local field office, typically 8-14 months after filing. Both you and your spouse must attend. The officer will:

  • Verify your identities
  • Ask about your relationship and marriage
  • Review your evidence of a bona fide marriage
  • Ask about your living situation, daily life, and future plans

If approved, your spouse receives a conditional green card (2-year validity) in the mail.

Removing Conditions: I-751

90 days before the 2-year green card expires, you must file Form I-751 (Petition to Remove Conditions on Residence) to convert the conditional green card to a permanent 10-year green card. This requires continued evidence that your marriage is genuine — tax returns filed jointly, shared lease or mortgage, children, insurance beneficiary designations, etc.

Common Post-Arrival Mistakes to Avoid

1. Waiting Too Long to Get Married

Don't procrastinate. Life gets busy, things come up, and suddenly you're at day 75 scrambling to find an officiant. Get the legal ceremony done in the first month.

2. Not Filing AOS Quickly After Marriage

Every day you wait to file AOS is a day later your spouse gets their work permit. File within 1-2 weeks of the wedding.

3. Spouse Traveling Before Advance Parole

This is a critical error. If your spouse leaves the U.S. before receiving Advance Parole, the green card application is considered abandoned. Do not travel internationally until the AP card is in hand.

4. Spouse Working Before EAD

Working without authorization is an immigration violation that can jeopardize the entire green card application. No matter how tempting, wait for the EAD card.

5. Not Building Bona Fide Marriage Evidence

Start building evidence of your genuine marriage from day one:

  • Open a joint bank account
  • Add your spouse to your lease or mortgage
  • Add your spouse to your health insurance
  • Update beneficiary designations (401k, life insurance)
  • File taxes jointly (if married before December 31)
  • Take photos together at family events and holidays

You'll need this evidence for the AOS interview and later for the I-751 conditions removal.

6. Forgetting About the I-751

Mark your calendar: 90 days before the conditional green card expires, file I-751. Missing this deadline puts your spouse at risk of losing their green card.

7. Not Getting a Social Security Number

Your spouse can apply for a Social Security number as part of the EAD application (check the box on I-765). They'll need it for taxes, employment, and building credit. If they didn't check the box, they can visit a Social Security office with their EAD card after it arrives.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens if you don't marry within 90 days on a K-1 visa?

Your fiancé's K-1 status expires and they become unlawfully present. They must leave the country or face deportation, bars on future entry, and loss of immigration eligibility. There is no extension available.

Can you extend the 90-day K-1 visa period?

No. The 90-day period cannot be extended under any circumstances. No waiver, no exception, no form. The marriage must be legally performed before the 90 days expire.

Does a courthouse wedding count?

Yes. Any legally valid marriage counts, including a courthouse civil ceremony. You do not need a religious ceremony or a large wedding. Many K-1 couples have a simple civil ceremony to meet the deadline and plan a larger celebration later.

Can my fiancé work after arriving on a K-1 visa?

Not until they receive an Employment Authorization Document (EAD), which is filed as part of Adjustment of Status after marriage. The EAD typically takes 3-6 months. Plan financially for this gap. Read more about K-1 visa costs including post-arrival expenses.

Can my fiancé leave the U.S. during the 90 days?

Technically yes, but the K-1 is a single-entry visa. If your fiancé leaves the U.S. before marriage, they cannot re-enter on the same K-1 visa. They would need a new visa. Don't risk it.

How soon after marriage should I file Adjustment of Status?

File within 1-2 weeks of the wedding. There's no strict deadline, but prompt filing protects your spouse's status and starts the work permit clock sooner.

What if my fiancé's 90 days fall over a holiday period?

County clerk offices and courthouses are closed on holidays and weekends. Factor this into your planning. If day 90 falls on Christmas Day, you need to be married before then. Don't cut it close.

Do we need a lawyer for Adjustment of Status?

For straightforward cases, a document preparation service can handle the AOS paperwork at a fraction of attorney costs. If your case has complications (criminal history, prior immigration issues), consult an attorney. See our AOS services.

Can we have a destination wedding outside the U.S.?

No. Your fiancé must remain in the U.S. during the 90-day period (the K-1 is single-entry). The wedding must take place in the United States. You can plan an international celebration after your spouse receives Advance Parole or their green card.
