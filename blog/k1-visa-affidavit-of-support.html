The I-134 Affidavit of Support (officially called the "Declaration of Financial Support") is one of the most misunderstood parts of the K-1 fiancé visa process. It's the U.S. government's way of making sure your fiancé(e) won't become a public charge after arriving in America.

Get it wrong, and your fiancé(e)'s visa can be denied at the interview. Get it right, and it's just another form in the stack.

This guide explains exactly what the I-134 is, what income you need, what documents to attach, and how to handle every tricky situation — from self-employment to joint sponsors to military service.

What Is the I-134 Affidavit of Support?

The I-134 is a form filed by the U.S. citizen petitioner (you) declaring that you have sufficient income or assets to financially support your fiancé(e) when they arrive in the United States. It tells the U.S. government: "I can take care of this person financially. They won't need government assistance."

Key facts about the I-134:

  • Who files it: The U.S. citizen petitioner
  • When it's needed: Your fiancé(e) brings it to the visa interview at the U.S. embassy/consulate
  • Filing fee: None (the I-134 has no fee)
  • Legal weight: It's a declaration of intent, not a legally binding contract (that comes later with the I-864)
  • Form number: USCIS Form I-134, Declaration of Financial Support

Important: Even though the I-134 is technically less binding than the I-864, consular officers take it very seriously. An insufficient I-134 is one of the top reasons for K-1 visa interview complications. Treat it as if it were legally binding.

This is one of the most common points of confusion. Here's the breakdown:

You'll need both. The I-134 for the K-1 visa interview, and the I-864 later when you file for Adjustment of Status after the wedding. Start preparing the I-134 well before the interview date.

2026 Income Requirements (Federal Poverty Guidelines)

The U.S. government requires that the K-1 petitioner's income meets or exceeds 125% of the federal poverty guidelines for their household size. Here are the 2026 numbers:

For most K-1 petitioners with no dependents, the magic number is $25,550/year. That's the minimum annual income you need to show for a household of 2 (you plus your fiancé).

Alaska and Hawaii: Poverty guidelines are higher. Alaska household of 2 = $31,938; Hawaii household of 2 = $29,388.

This income is based on your current annual income, not just what you earned last year. If you recently got a raise or new job, your current salary may qualify even if last year's tax return shows less.

How to Calculate Your Household Size

Your household size for the I-134 includes:

  1. You (the petitioner) — always counted
  2. Your fiancé(e) (the beneficiary) — always counted
  3. Your dependent children (any age, if you claim them on taxes or they live with you)
  4. Any other dependents you claimed on your most recent tax return
  5. Your fiancé(e)'s children if they'll be accompanying them on a K-2 visa
  6. Anyone else for whom you've previously signed an I-864 (if you sponsored someone before)

Example: You're a single person sponsoring your fiancé who has one child coming on a K-2 visa. Your household size = 3 (you + fiancé + child). Required income: $32,275.

Example: You have one child from a previous marriage and are sponsoring your fiancé with no children. Household size = 3 (you + your child + fiancé). Required income: $32,275.

Documents to Include with I-134

The I-134 form alone is not enough. You need to back it up with financial evidence. Here is the complete list of documents to attach:

Required Documents

  • Most recent federal tax return (Form 1040) — or IRS tax transcript. Include all schedules
  • W-2s or 1099s from the most recent tax year
  • Recent pay stubs — at least 3-6 months of pay stubs from your current employer
  • Employment verification letter — on company letterhead, stating your position, salary, and start date
  • Bank statements — most recent 3-6 months for all checking and savings accounts

Strongly Recommended Documents

  • Previous year's tax return — 2-3 years of returns show income stability
  • Investment account statements — brokerage, retirement accounts (401k, IRA)
  • Property deed or mortgage statement — if you own real estate
  • Vehicle title — if owned outright (shows assets)
  • Letter from a CPA or accountant — especially if self-employed

If You Don't Meet Income on Your Own

  • Joint sponsor's I-134 with their financial documents (see joint sponsor section below)
  • Asset documentation showing value of savings, investments, or property
  • Evidence of expected income changes — job offer letter, signed contract, etc.

Using Assets Instead of Income

If your income falls below the 125% poverty guideline threshold, you can supplement with assets. Here's how it works:

The 3x rule: Assets are valued at one-third of their net value for I-134 purposes. This means you need assets worth at least 3 times the shortfall between your income and the required minimum.

Example calculation:

  • Required income (household of 2): $25,550
  • Your actual income: $20,000
  • Shortfall: $5,550
  • Assets needed: $5,550 x 3 = $16,650 in net assets

What Counts as Assets

  • Cash in bank accounts (most liquid, most valued by consular officers)
  • Stocks, bonds, mutual funds (provide current statements)
  • Certificates of deposit (CDs)
  • Real estate equity (market value minus mortgage balance)
  • Retirement accounts (401k, IRA — though these are less favored since they can't be easily liquidated)

What Does NOT Count

  • Personal belongings (car, furniture, jewelry)
  • Life insurance cash value (generally)
  • Assets that are not easily converted to cash
  • Someone else's assets (unless they're a joint sponsor)

Joint Sponsors: When and How

If you don't meet the income or asset requirements on your own, a joint sponsor can help.

Who Can Be a Joint Sponsor?

  • Any U.S. citizen or lawful permanent resident
  • Must be 18 years or older
  • Must be domiciled in the United States
  • Does NOT have to be a family member (a friend, coworker, or anyone willing)
  • Must independently meet 125% of the poverty guidelines for their own household size PLUS the beneficiary

How Joint Sponsorship Works

  1. The joint sponsor fills out their own I-134 form
  2. They attach their own financial documents (tax returns, W-2s, pay stubs, bank statements)
  3. Their household size includes their own dependents plus your fiancé(e)
  4. Both your I-134 and the joint sponsor's I-134 go to the interview

Example: Your friend agrees to be joint sponsor. They are single with no dependents. Their household size for this purpose = 2 (themselves + your fiancé). They need to show $25,550 in income.

Can I have more than one joint sponsor? While the I-134 technically allows it, it's unusual and may raise questions. One qualified joint sponsor is better than multiple weak ones.

Important Note for Joint Sponsors

While the I-134 joint sponsorship is a declaration (not a binding contract), the joint sponsor will likely need to file an I-864 Affidavit of Support at the Adjustment of Status stage — which IS legally binding. Make sure your joint sponsor understands this future obligation.

Self-Employment and Irregular Income

Self-employed petitioners face extra scrutiny because income can be irregular and tax returns often show lower net income due to business deductions. Here's how to handle it:

Documents Self-Employed Petitioners Should Provide

  • 2-3 years of complete tax returns (including all schedules, especially Schedule C or Schedule K-1)
  • Year-to-date profit and loss statement
  • Business bank statements (3-6 months)
  • Personal bank statements (3-6 months)
  • 1099 forms from clients
  • CPA letter — this is highly recommended. Have your accountant write a letter confirming your annual income, that the business is active, and projecting current-year earnings
  • Business license or registration
  • Client contracts or invoices showing ongoing work

The Tax Deduction Problem

Many self-employed people have a high gross income but a low Adjusted Gross Income (AGI) because of legitimate business deductions. The consular officer looks at your AGI on your tax return, which can be misleadingly low.

Solutions:

  • Include a CPA letter explaining that your gross revenue is significantly higher than your AGI
  • Provide bank statements showing actual deposits that exceed the poverty guideline
  • Show current contracts or invoices demonstrating ongoing revenue
  • Supplement with assets if your AGI alone doesn't meet the threshold
  • Consider a joint sponsor as backup

Gig Workers, Freelancers, and 1099 Contractors

If you work through platforms (Uber, DoorDash, Upwork, Fiverr, etc.), you're self-employed in the eyes of the IRS. Follow the same guidance above. Provide platform earning statements in addition to tax documents.

Military Service Members

Active-duty U.S. military members get a significant advantage on the I-134:

Lower Income Threshold

Active-duty military only need to meet 100% of federal poverty guidelines (not 125%). For a household of 2 in 2026, that's $20,440 instead of $25,550.

Documents for Military Petitioners

  • Leave and Earnings Statement (LES) — most recent
  • DD-214 — if recently separated
  • Military orders — showing active-duty status
  • Tax returns — still recommended, though the LES often suffices
  • BAH/BAS documentation — housing and food allowances count as income

Important: BAH (Basic Allowance for Housing), BAS (Basic Subsistence Allowance), and other non-taxable military allowances count as income for I-134 purposes, even though they don't appear on your tax return. Include your LES which shows total compensation.

Deployed Service Members

If you're deployed, you can still file the I-134. Have a family member or the JAG office help prepare and mail the documents. Power of attorney may be needed for some steps. Military legal assistance offices are familiar with this process.

Common Mistakes That Cause Problems

These are the errors we see most frequently — and the ones most likely to cause delays or denials at the embassy interview:

  1. Submitting the I-134 without supporting documents. The form alone is not enough. You MUST include tax returns, pay stubs, bank statements, and employment verification. A bare I-134 will likely result in a Request for Evidence (RFE) or denial
  2. Using old financial documents. Pay stubs should be from the last 30-90 days. Bank statements from the last 3-6 months. Tax returns from the most recent filing year. Outdated documents suggest you may not currently meet the requirement
  3. Forgetting to include all household members. If you have children, dependents, or previously sponsored immigrants, they must be counted in your household size. Undercounting makes your income look sufficient when it may not be
  4. Not signing the form. The I-134 must be signed by the petitioner. Unsigned forms are automatically rejected
  5. Listing gross income instead of adjusted gross income. The relevant figure is your AGI from your tax return, not your gross salary. If your AGI is lower than the threshold due to deductions, supplement with additional evidence
  6. Leaving bank balances low at the time of statement. If you just paid a large bill and your account balance is unusually low, that's what the consular officer sees. Make sure your statements show consistent, sufficient balances
  7. Not getting the joint sponsor lined up in advance. Finding a qualified joint sponsor takes time. Don't wait until the week before the interview. Identify and prepare the joint sponsor's package early
  8. Mixing up the I-134 and I-864. The I-134 is for the K-1 visa interview. The I-864 is for Adjustment of Status after marriage. Using the wrong form causes delays
  9. Ignoring employment gaps. If your tax returns show a period of unemployment, include a brief explanation and show that you're currently employed and earning above the threshold
  10. Failing to include the petitioner's income when they recently started a new job. If you just started a new, higher-paying job, include the offer letter, first pay stubs, and an employment verification letter. Your current income matters more than last year's tax return

Step-by-Step: Filling Out the I-134

Here's a walkthrough of the key sections of the I-134 form:

Part 1: Information About the Sponsor (You)

  • Your full legal name, address, date of birth, and Social Security Number
  • Your citizenship status (must be U.S. citizen for K-1)
  • Your relationship to the beneficiary (fiancé/fiancée)

Part 2: Information About the Beneficiary

  • Your fiancé(e)'s full legal name, date of birth, country of citizenship
  • Any dependents accompanying them (K-2 children)

Part 3: Sponsor's Household Size

  • Count yourself, your fiancé(e), your dependents, their dependents (if accompanying), and anyone you've previously sponsored
  • This determines which poverty guideline row applies to you

Part 4: Sponsor's Employment and Income

  • Current employer name, address, your position, and salary
  • Self-employed? List your business name and nature of work
  • Annual income from employment
  • Additional income from other sources (rental, investments, etc.)

Part 5: Sponsor's Assets

  • Cash in savings and checking accounts
  • Value of stocks, bonds, and investments
  • Value of real estate (minus mortgage)
  • Other significant assets

Part 6: Sponsor's Liabilities

  • Mortgage balance
  • Car loans
  • Credit card debt
  • Student loans
  • Other debts

Signature

Sign and date the form. If filed electronically, follow USCIS guidelines for electronic signatures.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the minimum income for a K-1 visa in 2026?

For a household size of 2 (petitioner + fiancé), the minimum is $25,550/year. This is 125% of the 2026 federal poverty guidelines. Active-duty military only need $20,440 (100% of guidelines).

What's the difference between the I-134 and I-864?

The I-134 is a declaration of intent to support, used at the K-1 visa interview stage. The I-864 is a legally binding contract filed during Adjustment of Status after marriage. You'll need both at different stages of the process.

Can I use a joint sponsor?

Yes. Any U.S. citizen or permanent resident who meets the income requirements can serve as a joint sponsor. They fill out their own I-134 with their own financial documents. They don't need to be a family member.

Can I use assets instead of income?

Yes. Assets are valued at one-third of their net value. You need assets worth at least 3 times the difference between your actual income and the required minimum. Cash and liquid investments are preferred over real estate.

Do I need to submit tax returns with the I-134?

Yes. Submit your most recent federal tax return (or IRS tax transcript), W-2s, and recent pay stubs. While the I-134 technically requires less documentation than the I-864, consulates increasingly expect robust financial proof.

What if I'm self-employed?

Self-employed petitioners should provide 2-3 years of complete tax returns, a current profit and loss statement, business bank statements, 1099 forms, and a CPA letter confirming income. The key is proving consistent income above the threshold.

What happens if the I-134 is rejected?

If the consular officer finds the I-134 insufficient, they may issue a 221(g) refusal and request additional financial evidence. This is not a permanent denial — you can submit additional documents and the case will be reconsidered. Read more in our K-1 visa denial guide.

Can my fiancé(e)'s income count?

The beneficiary's (your fiancé's) income generally does not count on the I-134 since they are not yet in the U.S. However, if they have assets (savings, property) that can be documented, those can sometimes supplement the sponsor's income in the consular officer's evaluation.

What if I just started a new job?

A new, higher-paying job is actually good news. Provide your offer letter, employment contract, first pay stubs, and an employer verification letter showing your annual salary. Current income is what matters most, even if your most recent tax return shows lower earnings from the previous year.
