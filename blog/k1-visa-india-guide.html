If you're an American citizen engaged to someone in India, the K-1 fiancé visa is your fastest path to bringing them to the United States. India is one of the top countries of origin for K-1 visa applicants, and the process has specific nuances that every couple needs to understand.

K-1 Visa from India: Overview

The K-1 visa allows a U.S. citizen to bring their foreign fiancé(e) to the United States for the purpose of getting married. Once in the U.S., the couple must marry within 90 days, after which the foreign spouse files for Adjustment of Status to obtain a green card.

For Indian applicants, the process follows the same federal framework as any other country, but the Indian document system, consular post procedures, and cultural factors create unique considerations. Understanding these details upfront prevents delays and denials.

Key facts for India in 2026:

  • Primary interview location: U.S. Consulate General, Mumbai
  • Total timeline: 12-18 months from petition to visa issuance
  • Total cost: $2,800-$5,000 USD (approx. INR 2,35,000-4,20,000)
  • Approval rate: India has a historically strong approval rate for K-1 visas when documentation is thorough
  • No religious conversion required for any faith combination

Eligibility Requirements

Before starting the process, both the U.S. citizen petitioner and the Indian beneficiary must meet these requirements:

U.S. Citizen Petitioner Must:

  • Be a U.S. citizen (green card holders cannot file K-1 petitions)
  • Be legally free to marry (single, divorced, or widowed with documentation)
  • Have met the Indian fiancé(e) in person within the past 2 years
  • Intend to marry within 90 days of the fiancé(e)'s arrival in the U.S.
  • Meet income requirements (125% of federal poverty guidelines)

Indian Beneficiary Must:

  • Be legally free to marry under both Indian law and U.S. law
  • Have a valid Indian passport
  • Have no criminal record or inadmissibility issues
  • Pass the medical examination
  • Demonstrate a genuine relationship with the petitioner

Important for Indian applicants: If your fiancé was previously married, they must provide a divorce decree or death certificate. Under the Hindu Marriage Act, Muslim Personal Law, or other applicable laws, the divorce must be legally finalized. Religious divorces (such as triple talaq) that are not recognized by Indian civil courts are insufficient for U.S. immigration purposes.

U.S. Embassy & Consulate Processes in India

India has five U.S. diplomatic posts that handle visa services. Here's how K-1 visa processing works at each:

U.S. Consulate General, Mumbai (Primary Post)

Mumbai is the primary immigrant visa processing post for all of India. The vast majority of K-1 visa interviews are conducted here, regardless of which state the applicant lives in.

  • Address: C-49, G-Block, Bandra Kurla Complex, Mumbai 400051
  • Interview scheduling: Typically 4-8 weeks after NVC completes processing
  • Interview day: Arrive by 7:30 AM; interviews usually happen between 8:00 AM-11:00 AM
  • Processing time after interview: If approved, visa stamped and passport returned within 5-7 business days

U.S. Embassy, New Delhi

The New Delhi embassy handles some immigrant visa cases, particularly for applicants in North India. If your case is routed through Delhi, the process is similar to Mumbai.

  • Address: Shantipath, Chanakyapuri, New Delhi 110021
  • Note: Delhi has been expanding immigrant visa processing capacity in recent years

U.S. Consulate General, Chennai

Chennai processes immigrant visas for applicants in Tamil Nadu and neighboring southern states. K-1 cases may be scheduled here based on the applicant's residence.

  • Address: 220, Anna Salai, Gemini Circle, Chennai 600006

U.S. Consulate General, Hyderabad

Hyderabad has become an increasingly important post due to the large tech industry population and growing immigrant visa caseload.

  • Address: Paigah Palace, 1-8-323, Chiran Fort Lane, Begumpet, Hyderabad 500003

U.S. Consulate General, Kolkata

Kolkata serves applicants in eastern India, including West Bengal, Bihar, Jharkhand, and northeastern states.

  • Address: 5/1, Ho Chi Minh Sarani, Kolkata 700071

Which post will handle your case? The National Visa Center (NVC) assigns the interview location based on the applicant's address. You can request a transfer to a different post, but this may add weeks to processing time. In most cases, Mumbai is the default.

Indian Documents You Need

Indian documents require specific handling. Here's the complete list with guidance on where to get each one:

1. Indian Passport

  • Must be valid for at least 6 months beyond the intended entry date to the U.S.
  • If expired or expiring soon, renew through Passport Seva Kendra (apply at passportindia.gov.in)
  • Standard renewal takes 1-3 weeks; tatkal (urgent) service available for higher fee
  • Cost: INR 1,500 (standard) or INR 3,500 (tatkal) for a 36-page booklet

2. Birth Certificate

  • Issued by the Municipal Corporation (Nagar Nigam/Nagar Palika) of the city/town of birth
  • Must be an official certified copy with the registrar's stamp and signature
  • If you were born before birth registration was common, you may need a Non-Availability Certificate from the municipality, accompanied by a school leaving certificate or an affidavit of birth
  • The birth certificate must include: full name, date of birth, place of birth, parents' names
  • Cost: INR 50-500 depending on municipality

3. Police Clearance Certificate (PCC)

  • Obtained from the Passport Seva Kendra (PSK) or Regional Passport Office
  • Apply online at passportindia.gov.in and schedule an appointment
  • Bring your current passport, Aadhaar card, and address proof
  • Police verification is conducted at your local police station
  • Processing time: 1-3 weeks (can take longer depending on police station efficiency)
  • Cost: INR 500
  • Important: If your fiancé has lived in another country for 6+ months since age 16, they need a PCC from that country too

4. Court/Civil Records

  • Divorce decree: Final decree from the family court (not the interim order). Must show both parties' names and the date the divorce became final
  • Death certificate: If previous spouse is deceased, from the municipal corporation
  • Court marriage certificate: If previously married under the Special Marriage Act

5. Educational Documents

  • 10th and 12th class mark sheets (may be requested as supplementary identity documents)
  • Degree certificates if applicable

6. Photographs

  • 2 passport-style photos meeting U.S. visa specifications: 2x2 inches, white background, taken within 6 months
  • Available at most photo studios near the consulate or PSK; cost: INR 100-300

7. Relationship Evidence

This is critical. You must prove the relationship is genuine. For Indian applicants, strong evidence includes:

  • Photos together during visits to India or the U.S.
  • WhatsApp, video call, and messaging logs
  • Flight tickets and hotel bookings from visits
  • Engagement ceremony (roka/sagai) photos and invitations
  • Letters or affidavits from family members who have met both partners
  • Gift receipts, money transfer records
  • Joint travel itineraries or bookings

Translation Requirements

All documents not in English must include a certified English translation. Most Indian official documents (passport, PCC) are already in English or bilingual. However, birth certificates from smaller municipalities, Hindi or regional language divorce decrees, and religious marriage certificates typically need professional translation.

  • Translation must include a certification statement by the translator
  • Cost: INR 500-2,000 per document (approximately $6-$25 USD)

Medical Exam in India

The K-1 visa medical examination must be performed by a U.S. embassy-authorized panel physician. You cannot use your regular doctor.

Authorized Panel Physicians in India

The U.S. embassy maintains a list of authorized physicians in major Indian cities. As of 2026, panel physicians are available in:

  • Mumbai: Lilavati Hospital, Hinduja Hospital, and others
  • Delhi/NCR: Max Hospital, Apollo Hospital, and others
  • Chennai: Apollo Hospital and authorized clinics
  • Hyderabad: Apollo Hospital and authorized clinics
  • Kolkata: Authorized panel physicians in the city

What the Medical Exam Includes

  • Physical examination: Height, weight, vision, hearing, general health assessment
  • Blood tests: Syphilis (RPR/VDRL), HIV
  • Chest X-ray: For tuberculosis screening (India is considered a TB-prevalent country, so this is mandatory)
  • Vaccination review: CDC-required vaccines including MMR, Tdap, Varicella, Hepatitis B, and others

What to Bring

  • Passport
  • 2 passport-style photos
  • Vaccination records (any Indian immunization records you have)
  • DS-160 confirmation page
  • Panel physician appointment confirmation

Hindu, Muslim, Sikh & Interfaith Marriage Considerations

The K-1 visa itself has no religious requirements. You can be of any faith (or none) and qualify. However, Indian marriage traditions and legal frameworks create specific situations couples should know about:

Hindu Marriage Considerations

  • Hindu marriages in the U.S. are legally valid when performed by an authorized officiant and a valid marriage license is obtained from the county
  • A traditional Hindu ceremony (with a pandit, saat phere, etc.) is fine as the religious ceremony, but you still need the civil marriage license
  • Engagement ceremonies (roka, sagai, ring ceremony) count as strong relationship evidence. Bring photos and any invitation cards to the interview
  • If the family arranged the introduction, be prepared to explain how the relationship developed beyond the initial meeting

Muslim Marriage Considerations

  • A nikah performed in the U.S. can satisfy the 90-day marriage requirement, as long as a valid civil marriage license is also obtained
  • The meher (dower) agreement and nikah-nama can be presented as relationship evidence
  • If previously divorced: a divorce recognized by Indian civil courts is required. Provide the family court decree, not just a religious divorce certificate
  • Polygamy is not recognized under U.S. law. The petitioner and beneficiary must both be in a position to enter a monogamous marriage

Sikh Marriage Considerations

  • An Anand Karaj ceremony in the U.S. can serve as the religious component, paired with a civil marriage license
  • The Anand Marriage Act certificate from India (if previously married in a Gurdwara) is a valid document for immigration
  • Engagement (kurmai/rokna) ceremonies with family involvement are excellent relationship evidence

Interfaith Couples

  • There is absolutely no requirement that both partners share the same religion
  • In India, interfaith marriages are registered under the Special Marriage Act, 1954. If your fiancé was previously married interfaith in India, the registered certificate under this act is required
  • If family opposition exists, be prepared to demonstrate that the relationship is genuine and entered into freely. The consular officer will assess the relationship's authenticity

Interview Tips for India

The consular interview is the final major hurdle. Here are tips specific to Indian K-1 visa interviews:

Before the Interview

  • Organize documents in a folder: Use labeled tabs or dividers. Indian consulates process high volumes, and organized files make a positive impression
  • Bring originals AND copies of every document
  • Practice answering questions about how you met, the timeline of your relationship, wedding plans, and future living arrangements
  • Review our K-1 visa interview questions guide for the most common questions

Day of the Interview

  • Arrive early: Lines at Indian consulates can be long. Arrive 30-60 minutes before your appointment
  • No electronics inside: You cannot bring phones, laptops, or smart watches into the consulate. Leave them with a companion or use a nearby phone storage service (these are common near Indian consulates)
  • Dress professionally: Business casual or formal attire. Conservative and neat
  • Bring water and snacks: You may wait several hours. However, these must be left outside the building

During the Interview

  • Speak clearly and confidently in English. If your fiancé is more comfortable in Hindi or another language, they can request an interpreter, but English proficiency helps
  • Be honest: Consular officers are trained to detect inconsistencies. If you don't know an answer, say so rather than guessing
  • Explain arranged introductions properly: If your families introduced you, explain that while the initial introduction was arranged, the relationship developed organically and you both chose each other willingly
  • Show genuine knowledge of each other: Know your partner's daily life, job, family, hobbies, and future plans together
  • Don't over-explain: Answer the question that was asked. Volunteering excessive information can raise additional questions

Common Questions at Indian Consulates

  1. How did you and your fiancé(e) meet?
  2. When did you meet in person? How many times?
  3. Who proposed? How?
  4. What are your wedding plans in the U.S.?
  5. Where will you live after the wedding?
  6. What does your fiancé(e) do for work?
  7. Have you met each other's families?
  8. How do you communicate? How often?
  9. What is your fiancé(e)'s address in the U.S.?
  10. Did your families approve of the relationship?

After Visa Approval

Once the interview is successful:

  1. Visa stamped in passport: Returned via courier within 5-7 business days
  2. Sealed immigrant packet: Given to you at the consulate. Do NOT open this. Hand it to the CBP officer at the U.S. port of entry
  3. USCIS immigrant fee: Pay the $235 USCIS Immigrant Fee online before traveling
  4. Book your flight: The K-1 visa is valid for 6 months from issuance. Enter the U.S. before it expires
  5. At the U.S. port of entry: Present your passport with visa, sealed packet, and any documents requested
  6. Marry within 90 days: This is a legal requirement. You cannot extend the 90-day window. See our 90-day rule guide
  7. File Adjustment of Status (I-485): After marriage, file for a green card. Include I-765 (work permit) and I-131 (travel document) in the same package

Common Mistakes Indian K-1 Applicants Make

  1. Not getting the PCC early enough: Police verification in India can take weeks depending on your local police station. Start the PCC process as soon as USCIS approves the I-129F
  2. Using a non-panel physician: The medical exam must be from a U.S. embassy-approved panel physician. Exams from regular doctors are not accepted
  3. Weak relationship evidence: "We talk on the phone" is not enough. Bring printed WhatsApp conversations, video call screenshots, trip photos, and family meeting evidence. Read our relationship evidence guide
  4. Incomplete birth certificate: If your municipality did not register the birth, you need both a Non-Availability Certificate AND an alternative document (school leaving certificate or sworn affidavit)
  5. Not disclosing previous marriages: India's complex marriage laws mean some people are unsure of their marital status. Get absolute clarity before applying. Undisclosed prior marriages are grounds for denial
  6. Relying only on arranged marriage explanation: Consular officers need to see that a real relationship exists beyond the family arrangement. Show communication history, visits, and mutual knowledge of each other
  7. Ignoring the Affidavit of Support: The U.S. petitioner must prove they can financially support the fiancé(e). See our I-134 Affidavit of Support guide

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does the K-1 visa take from India in 2026?

The total K-1 visa timeline from India is approximately 12-18 months. USCIS petition processing takes 6-10 months, NVC processing takes 4-8 weeks, and the U.S. Consulate in Mumbai or other posts schedules interviews within 4-8 weeks after NVC completion.

How much does a K-1 visa from India cost?

The total cost is approximately $2,800-$5,000 USD (INR 2,35,000-4,20,000). This includes U.S. government fees of $2,380, medical exam costs of $170-$325, Indian document fees of $50-$200, and optional professional services. See our detailed cost breakdown.

Which U.S. consulate in India handles K-1 interviews?

The U.S. Consulate General in Mumbai handles the majority of K-1 visa interviews. Cases may also be processed in New Delhi, Chennai, Hyderabad, or Kolkata depending on the applicant's residence.

Do I need to convert to my fiancé's religion?

No. The K-1 visa has no religious requirements. Hindu, Muslim, Sikh, Christian, and interfaith couples are all equally eligible. You must simply demonstrate a genuine relationship and intend to legally marry within 90 days.

What Indian documents do I need for a K-1 visa?

You need a valid Indian passport, birth certificate from the municipal corporation, police clearance certificate (PCC) from the Passport Seva Kendra, any divorce/death certificates if previously married, medical exam results, passport photos, and comprehensive relationship evidence.

Can my Indian fiancé(e) work in the U.S. on a K-1 visa?

Not immediately. After arrival, marriage, and filing for Adjustment of Status (I-485), your spouse can file Form I-765 for an Employment Authorization Document (EAD). The EAD typically arrives within 3-5 months of filing.

What if my K-1 visa from India is denied?

Common denial reasons include insufficient relationship evidence, financial support concerns, or document issues. You can reapply after addressing the deficiency. Read our K-1 visa denial guide for detailed next steps.

Can I visit my fiancé in India on a tourist visa while the K-1 is pending?

Yes. Your fiancé can continue living normally in India while the K-1 petition is pending. The U.S. citizen petitioner can visit India on their U.S. passport (no visa needed for stays under 180 days with an e-Visa). These visits actually strengthen your relationship evidence.
