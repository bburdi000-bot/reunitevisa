Haiti presents unique challenges for K-1 fiance visa applicants in 2026. The security situation has limited U.S. Embassy operations in Port-au-Prince, and policy changes have added complexity for Haitian nationals. Despite these challenges, the K-1 process remains available, and thousands of Haitian-American couples are navigating it.

This guide covers the Haiti-specific K-1 process in 2026, including where interviews are being conducted, policy impacts, and what documents you need. Given the fluid situation, professional guidance is strongly recommended for Haitian K-1 applicants. Start with our K-1 visa requirements guide.

Haiti K-1 Visas: Current Situation

  • Embassy on Ordered Departure. The U.S. Embassy in Port-au-Prince is on reduced operations, impacting routine consular services including nonimmigrant visa services.
  • Nassau designated for processing. The U.S. Embassy in Nassau, The Bahamas has been designated to conduct visa services for residents of Haiti.
  • 2026 proclamation. Presidential Proclamation 10998 may restrict visa issuance for Haitian nationals.
  • French/Creole documents. Haitian documents are in French or Haitian Creole and require certified English translations.
  • Document accessibility challenges. Obtaining documents from Haitian government offices may be difficult due to the security situation.

2026 Policy Changes

Presidential Proclamation 10998, effective January 1, 2026, suspended or limited entry and visa issuance to nationals of countries including Haiti. Key points:

  • Applicants may still submit visa applications and attend scheduled interviews.
  • However, applicants may be ineligible for visa issuance or admission to the United States under the proclamation.
  • This policy is subject to change through legal challenges and executive action.
The combination of embassy operational limitations and policy changes makes professional legal guidance essential for Haitian K-1 applicants. Contact us for a consultation.

Processing Location

Due to the security situation in Port-au-Prince, K-1 visa interviews for Haitian residents may be conducted at the U.S. Embassy in Nassau, The Bahamas. Check the embassy websites for the most current processing location and instructions:

  • U.S. Embassy in Haiti: ht.usembassy.gov
  • U.S. Embassy in The Bahamas: bs.usembassy.gov

This means Haitian applicants may need to travel to Nassau for the interview, adding significant travel costs and logistical complexity.

Police Certificate: Certificat de Bonne Vie et Moeurs

The Haitian police certificate is the Certificat de Bonne Vie et Moeurs (Certificate of Good Life and Morals), issued by the Haitian National Police (Police Nationale d'Haiti) or the local Tribunal de Paix (Justice of the Peace court).

Given the security situation, obtaining this document may require planning and flexibility. Contact the embassy for guidance on alternative procedures if your local office is inaccessible.

Medical Examination

If interviews are conducted in Nassau, the medical exam may also need to be completed in The Bahamas with an embassy-designated panel physician there. Follow the specific instructions from the processing embassy.

Estimated cost: $200-$400 depending on location and vaccinations needed.

Interview Tips

  • Confirm your interview location. Verify whether your interview is in Port-au-Prince or Nassau before making travel plans.
  • Bring all original documents. Plan carefully given the difficulty of obtaining replacements.
  • Relationship evidence. Photos, communication records, travel records, and testimony.
  • Financial documentation is critical. Strong I-134 with evidence of U.S. petitioner's income.
  • Be prepared for policy questions. Officers may discuss how the current proclamation affects your case.

Common Issues

  • Policy restrictions on visa issuance. The current proclamation may prevent visa issuance even after interview approval.
  • Document accessibility. The security situation may make it difficult to obtain documents.
  • Travel logistics. If interviews are in Nassau, travel costs and logistics increase significantly.
  • Financial concerns. See our I-134 guide.

After the Visa

  1. Travel to the U.S. within 6 months.
  2. Marry within 90 days. See our 90-day rule guide.
  3. File Adjustment of Status (I-485, $1,440).
  4. Apply for work authorization (EAD).
