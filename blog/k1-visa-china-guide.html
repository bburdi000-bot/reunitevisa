K-1 Visa from China: Complete 2026 Guide

Last updated: March 2026·14 min read

Quick Answer

The K-1 visa process from China takes 13-18 months and costs $3,000-$4,800 total. All interviews are at the U.S. Consulate in Guangzhou, regardless of where your fiance lives in China. Chinese applicants need a notarized birth certificate from a local notary public office, a police certificate from the Public Security Bureau, and a medical exam. All Chinese documents require certified English translations.

China is a major source of K-1 fiance visa petitions. The growing number of Chinese-American relationships, international business connections, and educational exchanges create a steady flow of cross-border couples. The U.S. Consulate in Guangzhou is the sole processor of all immigrant and K visas for mainland China.

This guide covers everything specific to the Chinese K-1 visa process in 2026 — from the notarized document system to the Guangzhou consulate procedures and the Great Firewall's impact on communication evidence. If you haven't confirmed eligibility, start with our K-1 visa requirements guide.

Why China Is Unique for K-1 Visas

  • Guangzhou-only processing. All K-1 interviews happen at the U.S. Consulate in Guangzhou, regardless of where the applicant lives. Applicants from Beijing, Shanghai, Chengdu, or anywhere else must travel to Guangzhou.
  • Notarized document system. Chinese civil documents must be obtained from local notary public offices (gongzheng chu) rather than direct government registries. This is unique to China's legal system.
  • Hukou (household registration) system. China's hukou system ties many civil documents to the applicant's registered city, which may differ from where they currently live.
  • Communication challenges. China's internet restrictions affect platforms like Google, Facebook, and WhatsApp. Couples often use WeChat, which is important for communication evidence.
  • 2-3 month Guangzhou wait. Once the case arrives at Guangzhou, the interview is typically scheduled 2-3 months later.

U.S. Consulate in Guangzhou

All K-1 visa interviews in China are conducted at the U.S. Consulate General in Guangzhou. This is the only U.S. consular post in China that handles immigrant visa processing, despite the U.S. having an embassy in Beijing and consulates in Shanghai, Chengdu, Shenyang, and Wuhan.

The Process

  1. Case arrives from NVC to Guangzhou.
  2. Wait for interview scheduling. Typically 2-3 months after case arrival.
  3. Complete DS-160 and pay the visa fee.
  4. Gather notarized documents from local notary public offices.
  5. Medical exam 1-2 weeks before the interview at a consulate-designated clinic.
  6. Interview at Guangzhou consulate.

Important: Chinese birth certificates must be issued by a local notary public office (not a hospital) and must include the date of birth, place of birth, and names of both parents. If your birth was not properly registered, contact the notary office for guidance.

Police Certificate: Certificate of No Criminal Record

Chinese applicants need a Certificate of No Criminal Record (Wu Fanzui Jilu Zhengming) from the Public Security Bureau (PSB) in the city where they are registered (hukou location).

How to Get It

  1. Visit the local PSB office where your hukou is registered.
  2. Bring your national ID card (Shenfen Zheng) and hukou booklet.
  3. Processing time: Typically 3-7 business days, but can vary by city.
  4. Cost: CNY 50-200 (~$7-$28).
  5. If you have lived in multiple cities: You may need certificates from each PSB where you resided for 6+ months.

Medical Examination

Complete the medical exam at a consulate-designated clinic in China, typically 1-2 weeks before the interview. The consulate will provide specific clinic information.

What is included:

  • Complete physical examination
  • Chest X-ray (TB screening)
  • Blood tests
  • Vaccination verification (DT/DTP/DTaP, Td, Polio, Measles, Mumps, Rubella, and others)

Cost: approximately $200-$400 (CNY 1,400-2,800). Bring vaccination records from childhood to reduce costs. Results come in a sealed envelope.

Interview Tips for Guangzhou

  • Plan your Guangzhou trip. If your fiance lives far from Guangzhou, budget for flights/train, hotel (2-3 nights), and local transportation.
  • WeChat communication evidence. Since WhatsApp, Facebook, and Google are restricted in China, most couples communicate via WeChat. Print WeChat conversation histories, screenshots of video calls, and shared moments as relationship evidence.
  • Bring physical evidence. Photos with dates and locations, travel records (flight tickets, hotel receipts), greeting cards, and gifts.
  • Language considerations. If you and your fiance communicate in different languages, be prepared to explain how you communicate. Interpreters may be available at the consulate.
  • Know your petitioner's details. Address, employer, salary, family members, daily routine.
  • Previous visa history. If your fiance has previously been denied a U.S. visa (tourist or otherwise), be prepared to discuss it honestly.

Common Issues and Denial Reasons

  • Communication evidence gaps. Due to China's internet restrictions, couples may lack the kind of communication evidence (emails, social media interactions) that is common from other countries. Supplement with WeChat records, phone call logs, and travel documentation.
  • Notarization issues. Birth certificates not from a notary public office will be rejected. Hospital birth records alone are insufficient.
  • Hukou complications. If your fiance's hukou is in a different city from where they live, they may need to return to their hukou city for some documents.
  • Language barrier concerns. If the couple cannot communicate in a shared language, this is a significant red flag for consular officers.
  • Financial insufficiency. See our I-134 guide.

After the Visa

  1. Travel to the U.S. within 6 months.
  2. Marry within 90 days. See our 90-day rule guide.
  3. File Adjustment of Status (I-485, $1,440).
  4. Apply for work authorization (EAD).

Frequently Asked Questions

Where is the interview?

U.S. Consulate General in Guangzhou. All immigrant visas for mainland China are processed here.

How long does it take?

Approximately 14-18 months. The Guangzhou wait (2-3 months after case arrival) adds time compared to some other countries.

What police certificate do I need?

Certificate of No Criminal Record from the Public Security Bureau where your hukou is registered.

What about documents from a notary public office?

Chinese birth certificates must be notarized (gongzheng) by a local notary public office. Hospital records alone are not accepted.

Can I use WeChat as relationship evidence?

Yes. Print WeChat conversations, video call screenshots, and shared moments. Given China's internet restrictions, WeChat is the primary communication platform and consular officers understand this.

Do Chinese documents need translation?

Yes. All Chinese documents require certified English translations.
