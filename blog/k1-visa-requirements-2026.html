K-1 Visa Requirements 2026: Do You Qualify?

Last updated: March 2026 · 14 min read

Quick Answer

To qualify for a K-1 fiance visa in 2026, the petitioner must be a U.S. citizen (not a green card holder), both parties must be legally free to marry, you must have met in person within the past 2 years, you must intend to marry within 90 days of arrival, and the petitioner must earn at least 125% of the federal poverty guidelines ($25,550/year for a household of two).

Before you spend a single dollar on the K-1 visa process, you need to know one thing: do you actually qualify?

The K-1 fiance visa has specific eligibility requirements that both the U.S. citizen petitioner and the foreign fiance must meet. Miss even one, and your petition will be denied — along with the non-refundable filing fees.

This guide covers every K-1 visa requirement in 2026, including the edge cases and gray areas that trip people up. By the end, you'll know exactly where you stand.

Here is the quick version. You must meet all five of these requirements to qualify for a K-1 fiance visa:

Now let's break each one down in detail.

Requirement #1: The Petitioner Must Be a U.S. Citizen

This is the most fundamental requirement and the one that catches people off guard: only U.S. citizens can file a K-1 visa petition. Lawful permanent residents (green card holders) cannot.

If you are a green card holder, you have two options:

  • Naturalize first. If you are eligible for U.S. citizenship, complete the naturalization process and then file the K-1 petition.
  • Use a different visa category. Green card holders can sponsor a spouse through the F-2A category, but this requires marrying abroad first. You may also want to compare the K-1 vs CR-1 visa routes.

What counts as proof of citizenship?

  • U.S. passport (current or expired)
  • U.S. birth certificate
  • Certificate of Naturalization (Form N-550 or N-570)
  • Certificate of Citizenship (Form N-560 or N-561)
  • Consular Report of Birth Abroad (FS-240)

You must include a copy of one of these documents with your I-129F petition.

Requirement #2: You Must Have Met in Person Within the Past 2 Years

USCIS requires that you and your fiance have physically met face-to-face at least once in the 2 years before filing the I-129F petition. This is not optional, and video calls do not count.

What counts as "meeting in person"?

Any face-to-face meeting where you were both physically present in the same location. It does not need to be a long visit. Even a single meeting for a few hours qualifies, as long as you can prove it happened.

How to prove it:

  • Passport stamps showing you traveled to their country (or vice versa)
  • Boarding passes and flight itineraries
  • Hotel receipts
  • Photos together with metadata showing date and location
  • Receipts from restaurants, activities, or events you attended together
  • Statements from friends or family who witnessed the meeting

The Two Narrow Exceptions

USCIS will waive the in-person meeting requirement only in these two situations:

  1. Cultural customs exception: Meeting in person before marriage would violate the strict, long-established customs of your fiance's culture or social practice. This is very narrowly applied and typically involves arranged marriages in specific cultural traditions.
  2. Extreme hardship exception: Meeting in person would cause extreme hardship to the U.S. citizen petitioner. "Extreme hardship" means far more than financial inconvenience. It typically requires documented medical conditions, physical disabilities, or situations where travel to the fiance's country poses genuine danger.
Both waivers are difficult to obtain. USCIS denies the vast majority of waiver requests. If at all possible, meet in person before filing.

Requirement #3: Both Parties Must Be Legally Free to Marry

Both you and your fiance must be legally eligible to enter into a marriage at the time of filing. This means:

  • Neither of you is currently married. Any prior marriages must have been legally terminated through divorce, annulment, or death of the former spouse.
  • You must have documentary proof. USCIS requires divorce decrees, annulment decrees, or death certificates for every prior marriage — for both the petitioner and beneficiary.

Common issues that cause delays:

  • Pending divorce: You cannot file the I-129F until the divorce is finalized. Even if your hearing is next week, USCIS will not accept a petition based on a future divorce date.
  • Foreign divorces: Divorces obtained abroad must be legally recognized in the jurisdiction where they were granted. Some countries (like the Philippines) do not allow divorce, which means an annulment must be obtained instead — a process that can take years.
  • Missing documents: If you cannot obtain a divorce decree (for example, the court records were destroyed), you will need to provide alternative evidence and a detailed explanation.

Requirement #4: You Must Intend to Marry Within 90 Days

The K-1 visa is specifically for couples who plan to marry in the United States. Both parties must have a genuine, bona fide intention to marry each other within 90 days of the beneficiary entering the U.S.

This is not just a bureaucratic checkbox. USCIS officers and consular officials will evaluate whether your relationship is genuine during the petition review and the embassy interview. They are trained to spot fraud.

How USCIS evaluates intent:

  • Your statements on the I-129F petition
  • Evidence of an ongoing, genuine relationship (photos, messages, call logs, travel records)
  • The consular officer's assessment during the visa interview
  • Consistency between your answers and your fiance's answers

What happens if you don't marry within 90 days?

If the 90-day deadline passes without a marriage, the K-1 beneficiary must leave the United States. There are no extensions. Overstaying is a serious immigration violation that can result in future visa denials, bars on reentry, and deportation proceedings.

Requirement #5: The Income Requirement (Affidavit of Support)

The U.S. citizen petitioner must demonstrate the financial ability to support their fiance so they will not become a "public charge." This is formalized through Form I-134, Affidavit of Support.

The income threshold is 125% of the Federal Poverty Guidelines for your household size. Here are the 2026 numbers:

Your household size includes: yourself, your fiance, any dependents you already support, your fiance's children who will be immigrating, and any other people you have previously sponsored.

What Income Counts?

  • Employment income: Wages, salary, tips (most common)
  • Self-employment income: Net income from your own business
  • Retirement income: Social Security, pensions, annuities
  • Investment income: Dividends, interest, rental income
  • Assets: If your income falls short, you can use assets worth at least 3x the difference (for U.S. citizens) to supplement

What If You Don't Meet the Income Requirement?

You have several options:

  1. Use a joint sponsor. Any U.S. citizen or permanent resident who meets the income threshold can co-sign as a joint sponsor. They take on the same legal obligation as you.
  2. Use qualifying assets. Cash savings, stocks, bonds, and property equity can make up the shortfall. The asset value must be at least 3x the amount your income falls short.
  3. Combine household income. If your fiance's children (or other dependents) have income, it may count toward your household total in some situations.
The I-134 is required for the K-1 visa interview. Later, when you file Adjustment of Status, you will complete the more binding I-864 Affidavit of Support. The income requirements are the same, but the I-864 is a legally enforceable contract.

Requirements for the Foreign Fiance (Beneficiary)

The foreign fiance must also meet specific requirements:

Admissibility Requirements

  • Valid passport: Must be valid for at least 6 months beyond the intended date of entry to the U.S.
  • No criminal inadmissibility: Certain criminal convictions (drug trafficking, crimes involving moral turpitude, multiple convictions with aggregate sentences of 5+ years) can make a person inadmissible.
  • No immigration violations: Prior deportations, overstays, or misrepresentation on visa applications can trigger bars on admission.
  • Medical exam: The beneficiary must pass a medical examination by a USCIS-approved panel physician showing they are free of communicable diseases of public health significance and have required vaccinations.
  • Police clearances: Required from every country where the beneficiary has lived for 6+ months since age 16.

Age Requirements

Both parties must be at least 18 years old. Even if the marriage would be legal in the beneficiary's home country at a younger age, U.S. immigration law requires both parties to be 18 or older.

Disqualifiers: What Can Get You Denied

Certain factors can disqualify you from the K-1 visa process entirely or trigger additional scrutiny and requirements:

For the U.S. Citizen Petitioner

Criminal record (IMBRA violations): Under the International Marriage Broker Regulation Act, certain offenses require mandatory disclosure and can result in denial:

  • Domestic violence, sexual assault, child abuse, stalking
  • Homicide or attempted homicide
  • Human trafficking
  • Kidnapping or false imprisonment
  • Three or more convictions for controlled substance offenses

Even if you have one of these convictions, it is not an automatic permanent disqualification. You can request a waiver by demonstrating that you pose no risk to the beneficiary. USCIS must notify the beneficiary of the record and receive their acknowledgment.

Serial petitioners: If you have filed two or more K-1 petitions in the past, or had a K-1 beneficiary arrive within the last 2 years, you must request an IMBRA waiver. USCIS will scrutinize whether your current relationship is genuine.

Immigration fraud history: Prior involvement in a fraudulent marriage or visa fraud can permanently bar you from filing future petitions.

For the Foreign Fiance (Beneficiary)

Prior immigration violations:

  • Overstay of 180+ days: Triggers a 3-year bar on reentry
  • Overstay of 1+ year: Triggers a 10-year bar on reentry
  • Prior deportation/removal: 5-year bar (10-year if aggravated felony)
  • Fraud or misrepresentation: Permanent bar (waiver available in limited cases)

Health-related grounds:

  • Communicable diseases of public health significance (active tuberculosis is the most common issue)
  • Lack of required vaccinations (can be resolved by getting vaccinated)
  • Physical or mental disorder with associated harmful behavior
  • Drug abuse or addiction

Security-related grounds:

  • Espionage, sabotage, or terrorism
  • Membership in the Communist Party or totalitarian party (with narrow exceptions)
  • Participants in Nazi persecution, genocide, or torture

Edge Cases and Special Situations

Same-Sex Couples

K-1 visas are fully available to same-sex couples following the Supreme Court's ruling in Obergefell v. Hodges (2015). The requirements are identical. However, if your fiance is from a country where same-sex relationships are criminalized, consular interviews may require additional sensitivity planning. Contact us to discuss strategy for these cases.

Previously Denied K-1 Petition

A prior denial does not permanently bar you from filing again, but you must address the reason for denial in your new petition. If the denial was for insufficient evidence, strengthen your evidence package. If it was for fraud, consult a professional immediately.

Large Age Differences

Significant age gaps (15+ years) don't disqualify you, but they increase scrutiny. USCIS and consular officers will look more carefully at whether the relationship is genuine. Prepare extra evidence: extensive communication records, photos from multiple visits, evidence of shared plans and integration into each other's families.

Short Relationship History

There is no minimum relationship length requirement, but very short relationships (a few weeks or months) will face intense scrutiny. If you met, got engaged, and are filing within a short timeframe, be prepared to convincingly demonstrate that the relationship is genuine.

Online Relationships with Late In-Person Meetings

Many modern couples meet online and have a lengthy digital relationship before meeting in person. This is perfectly fine as long as you did meet in person within the 2-year window before filing. Document your online relationship history thoroughly — screenshots of early messages, video call logs, and the progression of your relationship.

Fiance Has Children

If your fiance has unmarried children under 21, they can be included in the K-1 petition as K-2 beneficiaries. Each child must be listed on the I-129F and will need their own medical exam and interview. Their inclusion increases your household size for the income requirement.

Military Petitioners

Active-duty military members have the same requirements as civilian petitioners. However, some military bases have legal assistance offices that can help with the process, and certain fees may be waived for military personnel in specific programs. The income requirement is easier to meet since military housing and subsistence allowances count toward your total income.

Petitioner Living Abroad

The U.S. citizen petitioner must have a domicile in the United States. If you are currently living abroad (even for work), you must demonstrate that you maintain a U.S. domicile or that you intend to establish one before your fiance enters the country. Evidence includes: U.S. property ownership or lease, U.S. bank accounts, U.S. voter registration, U.S. tax returns filed as a resident.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the basic requirements for a K-1 visa in 2026?

The petitioner must be a U.S. citizen, both parties must be legally free to marry, you must have met in person within the past 2 years, you must intend to marry within 90 days of the beneficiary's arrival, and the petitioner must meet the income requirement of 125% of the federal poverty guidelines ($25,550 for a household of 2).

Can a green card holder file a K-1 visa petition?

No. Only U.S. citizens can petition for a K-1 fiance visa. Lawful permanent residents must use the IR-1/CR-1 spouse visa process instead, which requires marrying first abroad.

What is the income requirement for a K-1 visa in 2026?

The petitioner must demonstrate income of at least 125% of the federal poverty guidelines. For a household of 2 (you and your fiance), that is $25,550 per year in 2026. If you cannot meet this threshold, you can use a joint sponsor or qualifying assets to make up the difference.

What if we have never met in person?

You must have met your fiance in person at least once within the past 2 years before filing the I-129F petition. Waivers exist but are very rarely granted — only for strict cultural customs or extreme hardship situations. If possible, plan a trip to meet before filing.

Can I file a K-1 visa if I have a criminal record?

It depends on the offense. Under IMBRA, certain violent or sexual offenses must be disclosed and can trigger additional review. Drug trafficking convictions are also a serious barrier. However, many criminal records do not automatically disqualify you. A waiver may be available.

How many times can I file a K-1 visa petition?

There is no hard legal limit, but USCIS scrutinizes serial petitioners. If you have filed two or more K-1 petitions previously, or had a K-1 beneficiary arrive within the past 2 years, you must request an IMBRA waiver and provide additional evidence that your current relationship is genuine.
