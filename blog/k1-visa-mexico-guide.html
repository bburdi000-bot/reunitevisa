If your fiancé is in Mexico, you're in a unique position. Mexico's proximity to the United States means shorter flights, easier visits, and the ability to stay connected across the border — but the K-1 visa process itself is just as rigorous as any other country.

This guide covers everything specific to the K-1 visa process from Mexico: the Ciudad Juarez consulate, Mexican document requirements, the medical exam, interview preparation, and costs. If you're looking for the general K-1 process, start with our K-1 visa requirements guide.

K-1 Visa from Mexico: Overview

Here's the big picture. The K-1 visa process from Mexico follows the same steps as any other country, with a few Mexico-specific details:

  1. U.S. citizen files I-129F petition with USCIS (filed from the U.S.)
  2. USCIS approves the petition and sends it to the National Visa Center (NVC)
  3. NVC forwards the case to the U.S. Consulate in Ciudad Juarez
  4. Your fiancé gathers Mexican documents (birth certificate, police clearance, etc.)
  5. Medical exam with a panel physician in Mexico
  6. Interview at Ciudad Juarez consulate
  7. Visa issued — your fiancé enters the U.S. within 6 months
  8. Marry within 90 days of arrival, then file for Adjustment of Status
Mexico is one of the top 5 countries for K-1 visa applications. Ciudad Juarez processes thousands of K-1 cases annually and the consular staff is very experienced with fiance visa interviews.

Step 1: Filing the I-129F Petition

This step is the same regardless of your fiancé's country. The U.S. citizen petitioner files Form I-129F with USCIS, including:

  • Completed Form I-129F
  • Proof of U.S. citizenship
  • Proof of in-person meeting within the past 2 years
  • Relationship evidence (photos, communications, travel records)
  • Proof of legal ability to marry (divorce decrees if applicable)
  • Passport-style photos of both partners
  • Filing fee: $675 (check or money order)

Mexico-specific advantage: Because Mexico borders the U.S., many couples have extensive evidence of in-person meetings — weekend visits, border crossings, and shared trips. This is great for your petition. Include every visit with dates, locations, and evidence (passport stamps, I-94 records, photos).

Step 2: National Visa Center Processing

After USCIS approves your I-129F, the case is sent to the National Visa Center (NVC). The NVC assigns a case number and forwards the file to the U.S. Consulate General in Ciudad Juarez.

During this time, your fiancé should:

  • Begin gathering all required Mexican documents (see below)
  • Create a CEAC (Consular Electronic Application Center) account
  • Complete the DS-160 online visa application
  • Pay the $265 visa application fee
  • Schedule the medical exam

The NVC stage is typically the shortest — 4-8 weeks. Use this time wisely to prepare everything for the interview.

Step 3: Mexican Documents You'll Need

Here are the Mexico-specific documents your fiancé must bring to the interview:

Acta de Nacimiento (Birth Certificate)

Your fiancé needs a certified copy of their birth certificate from the Registro Civil (Civil Registry). Important details:

  • Must be a recent certified copy — not the original from years ago. Many consulates want a copy issued within the last 6-12 months.
  • Can be obtained in person at the local Registro Civil office or online through some state websites.
  • Cost: approximately $100-$300 MXN ($5-$15 USD).
  • If there are any name discrepancies between the birth certificate and other documents, get an acta de aclaracion (correction affidavit) before the interview.

Carta de No Antecedentes Penales (Police Clearance)

This is the Mexican equivalent of a police clearance certificate. Your fiancé needs one from every Mexican state where they have lived for 6 or more months since age 16.

  • Obtained from the state's Fiscalia General del Estado (State Attorney General's office) or the Secretaria de Seguridad Publica.
  • Some states offer online applications; others require an in-person visit.
  • Processing time: 1-5 business days depending on the state.
  • Cost: approximately $200-$800 MXN ($10-$40 USD) per state.
  • Validity: Most police clearances are valid for 3-6 months. Don't get them too early — time it so they're still valid on the interview date.

CURP (Clave Unica de Registro de Poblacion)

Mexico's unique population registry number. Your fiancé should print a current copy from the official CURP website (gob.mx/curp). This is free.

Military Service Card (Cartilla Militar)

Required for Mexican males who completed their military service. If your fiancé is a male who served, bring the original cartilla militar.

Divorce Decree or Death Certificate

If your fiancé was previously married, they need the official Mexican divorce decree (acta de divorcio) from the Registro Civil, or a death certificate if widowed.

Passport

Your fiancé needs a valid Mexican passport. If it's expired or expiring soon, renew it before the interview. The passport must be valid for at least 6 months beyond the planned date of entry to the U.S.

Translations

All documents in Spanish must be accompanied by certified English translations. The Ciudad Juarez consulate requires complete, certified translations — not informal summaries. Budget $30-$100 per document for professional translation.

Step 4: Medical Exam in Mexico

Your fiancé must complete a medical exam with a USCIS-authorized panel physician. For Mexico, the main options are:

Approved Panel Physicians

The U.S. Embassy maintains a list of approved panel physicians in Mexico. As of 2026, authorized clinics include locations in:

  • Ciudad Juarez — most convenient since the interview is here
  • Mexico City
  • Guadalajara
  • Monterrey

Check the U.S. Embassy website for the current approved list and make an appointment in advance — clinics can book up quickly.

What the Medical Exam Includes

  • Physical examination
  • Blood tests (including syphilis and HIV)
  • Chest X-ray (for tuberculosis screening)
  • Review of vaccination history
  • Required vaccinations (if missing any USCIS-required vaccines)

Cost: $150-$250 USD

This is the base cost. Additional vaccinations (if needed) can add $50-$200. Common vaccines required include MMR, Tdap, varicella, influenza (seasonal), and COVID-19. Bring any existing vaccination records (cartilla de vacunacion) to potentially reduce costs.

Timing

Schedule the medical exam after your fiancé receives the interview appointment letter but before the interview. The medical results are sealed in an envelope that your fiancé brings unopened to the interview. Results are valid for 6 months.

If your fiancé schedules the exam in Ciudad Juarez, they can often do the medical exam the day before the interview to minimize travel.

Step 5: Ciudad Juarez Consulate Interview

All K-1 visa interviews for Mexican citizens take place at the U.S. Consulate General in Ciudad Juarez, Chihuahua. This is the only consulate in Mexico that processes immigrant visas and fiance visas.

Before the Interview

  • Complete the DS-160 online and print the confirmation page
  • Pay the $265 visa application fee
  • Gather all required documents (see above)
  • Complete the medical exam
  • Arrange travel to Ciudad Juarez (if your fiancé doesn't live there)

What to Bring

  • Valid passport
  • DS-160 confirmation page
  • Interview appointment letter
  • Sealed medical exam envelope
  • 2 passport-style photos (5cm x 5cm)
  • Birth certificate (acta de nacimiento) with English translation
  • Police clearance(s) (carta de no antecedentes penales) with English translation
  • Divorce decree or death certificate (if applicable) with English translation
  • Evidence of relationship with the U.S. citizen petitioner
  • I-134 Affidavit of Support from the petitioner
  • Petitioner's tax returns, W-2s, and pay stubs

Interview Day: What to Expect

The interview is conducted in English, though Spanish interpreters are available. Here's what happens:

  1. Arrive early. Plan to arrive 15-30 minutes before your appointment. The consulate is at Paseo de la Victoria 3650, Partido Senecú.
  2. Security screening. No electronics (phones, tablets, laptops) are allowed inside. Leave them at your hotel or use a nearby storage service.
  3. Document review. A clerk will review your documents before the interview.
  4. The interview itself. Typically 10-20 minutes. The consular officer will ask about your relationship, how you met, your plans for marriage, and your fiancé's background.

Common Interview Questions at Ciudad Juarez

Prepare your fiancé for questions like these (see our full K-1 visa interview question guide):

  • How did you and your fiancé meet?
  • How often do you communicate? How?
  • When was the last time you saw each other in person?
  • What are your wedding plans?
  • Where will you live in the United States?
  • What does your fiancé do for work?
  • Have you ever been denied a U.S. visa before?
  • Have you ever been to the United States before? (Be honest — they have records)

After the Interview

Most K-1 visa applicants at Ciudad Juarez receive one of three outcomes:

  • Approved: You'll be told the visa will be ready for pickup or mailed. Typically 3-7 business days.
  • Administrative processing (221g): The officer needs more information or time. You'll receive a letter explaining what's needed.
  • Denied: Rare for K-1 visas with proper documentation. The officer will explain the reason.

Step 6: After Visa Approval

Once the K-1 visa is issued, your fiancé has 6 months to enter the United States. After entry:

  • You must marry within 90 days. This is a hard deadline. Read our complete guide to the 90-day rule.
  • File Adjustment of Status (I-485) after the wedding to apply for a green card.
  • File I-765 for work authorization — your fiancé cannot legally work until the EAD card arrives (3-6 months after filing).
  • File I-131 for travel authorization — your fiancé cannot leave the U.S. until Advance Parole is approved, or they risk abandoning the green card application.

Mexico proximity advantage: Many couples with a fiancé from Mexico choose to have the wedding in a U.S. border city so that Mexican family members with tourist visas can easily attend. Cities like El Paso, San Diego, Tucson, and McAllen are popular choices.

Mexico-Specific Tips for a Smooth K-1 Process

1. Be Honest About Prior U.S. Travel

Many Mexican citizens have previously visited the U.S. on tourist visas, border crossing cards, or may have even had unauthorized stays. The consulate has access to all entry and exit records. Do not lie or omit any prior U.S. travel. If your fiancé has a complicated immigration history, consider consulting an attorney before filing. See our guide on when you need a lawyer for a K-1 visa.

2. Document Border Crossings as Relationship Evidence

If your fiancé has a border crossing card (BCC) or tourist visa, the history of visits to see you is powerful relationship evidence. Pull I-94 arrival/departure records from the CBP website and include them in your petition.

3. Get Police Clearances from Every State

This catches many applicants off guard. If your fiancé lived in Jalisco for college, then moved to Chihuahua for work, they need police clearances from both states. Make a complete list of every state your fiancé has lived in since age 16.

4. Plan the Ciudad Juarez Trip Carefully

If your fiancé doesn't live in Ciudad Juarez, they'll need to travel there for the interview. Tips:

  • Book a hotel near the consulate for the night before
  • Schedule the medical exam 1-2 days before the interview if using a Ciudad Juarez clinic
  • The consulate area can be busy — scout the location the day before
  • No phones or electronics allowed in the consulate — plan accordingly

5. Prepare for the "How Did You Meet?" Question

U.S.-Mexico couples often meet through family connections, work, school, or trips across the border. Make sure your fiancé can clearly and consistently describe how you met, including specific dates and locations. Practice the story together before the interview.

6. Name Discrepancies Are Common — Fix Them Early

Mexican naming conventions (two surnames, maternal and paternal) sometimes create discrepancies between U.S. and Mexican documents. If your fiancé's name appears differently on their birth certificate versus their passport, get this resolved before filing. An acta de aclaracion from the Registro Civil can fix most issues.

7. Consider the K-1 vs CR-1 Decision

Because Mexico is so close to the U.S., some couples choose to get married in Mexico first and then file for a CR-1 spousal visa instead. This can be faster in some cases and gives your spouse a green card on arrival (no 90-day deadline). Read our K-1 vs CR-1 comparison to decide which is right for you.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does the K-1 visa take from Mexico?

The total process takes approximately 10-14 months from petition filing to visa issuance. USCIS processing takes 6-10 months, NVC takes 4-8 weeks, and the Ciudad Juarez consulate schedules interviews within 4-8 weeks.

Where is the K-1 visa interview for Mexico?

All K-1 visa interviews for Mexican citizens are conducted at the U.S. Consulate General in Ciudad Juarez, Chihuahua. This is the only consulate in Mexico that processes immigrant and fiance visas.

Does my Mexican fiancé need a police clearance?

Yes. Your fiancé needs a carta de no antecedentes penales from every Mexican state where they've lived for 6+ months since age 16. Obtained from each state's Fiscalia General or Secretaria de Seguridad Publica.

Can my fiancé visit me in the U.S. while the K-1 is pending?

Technically yes, if they have a valid tourist visa or border crossing card. However, this is risky. If CBP suspects your fiancé intends to stay (immigrant intent), they can be denied entry and this could negatively affect the K-1 application. Many immigration advisors recommend avoiding U.S. visits while the K-1 is pending.

Can my fiancé work in the U.S. after arriving on the K-1?

Not immediately. After arriving on the K-1, your fiancé cannot legally work until they receive an Employment Authorization Document (EAD) through the Adjustment of Status process. This typically takes 3-6 months after filing. Plan financially for this gap.

What if my fiancé previously overstayed a U.S. visa?

This is a serious complication. An overstay can trigger a 3-year or 10-year bar on entry to the U.S., depending on the duration. If your fiancé has an overstay in their history, consult an immigration attorney before filing. A waiver may be possible, but it requires legal expertise.

Can Mexican family members attend our wedding in the U.S.?

Family members with valid U.S. tourist visas or border crossing cards can attend the wedding. Those without visas would need to apply for B-1/B-2 tourist visas. Many couples choose to have their civil ceremony at a U.S. border city to make it easier for Mexican family to attend.
