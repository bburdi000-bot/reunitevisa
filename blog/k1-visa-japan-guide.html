K-1 Visa from Japan: Complete 2026 Guide

Last updated: March 2026·13 min read

Quick Answer

The K-1 visa process from Japan takes 12-16 months and costs $3,200-$5,000 total. Interviews are at the U.S. Embassy in Tokyo. Japanese applicants need a police certificate from their prefectural police headquarters (delivered in a sealed envelope), a Juminhyo (resident registration), and a medical exam. All Japanese documents require certified English translations.

Japan is a steady source of K-1 fiance visa petitions. The U.S. military presence in Japan, the large number of American expats living there, and strong cultural exchange between the two countries create many American-Japanese relationships. The Tokyo embassy runs a well-organized K-1 process, and Japanese bureaucratic systems, while detailed, produce high-quality documentation.

This guide covers the Japan-specific K-1 process in 2026, from the sealed police certificate requirement to the Juminhyo, Koseki Tohon, and medical exams. If you haven't confirmed eligibility, start with our K-1 visa requirements guide.

Why Japan Is Unique for K-1 Visas

  • U.S. military connections. A significant number of K-1 petitions from Japan involve U.S. military service members stationed at bases like Yokosuka, Okinawa, and Camp Zama.
  • Sealed police certificate. The Japanese police certificate must remain in a sealed envelope. If the seal is broken, the certificate is invalid and must be re-obtained.
  • Koseki Tohon (Family Register). Japan uses a family register system rather than individual birth certificates. The Koseki Tohon serves as both proof of birth and marital status.
  • Juminhyo requirement. A resident registration document showing current address is needed.
  • Meticulous documentation culture. Japanese applicants tend to have well-organized documents, which is an advantage in the K-1 process.

U.S. Embassy in Tokyo

K-1 visa interviews are conducted at the U.S. Embassy in Tokyo. The embassy processes immigrant visas for all of Japan.

The Process

  1. Case arrives from NVC. The Tokyo embassy receives the approved petition.
  2. Instructions sent. The embassy provides specific K-1 instructions and a document checklist.
  3. Complete DS-160 and pay the $265 visa fee.
  4. Gather Japanese documents including police certificate, Koseki Tohon, and Juminhyo.
  5. Medical exam with an embassy-accredited physician.
  6. Interview at the embassy with all original documents.

Police Certificate

The Japanese police certificate is obtained from your Prefectural Police Headquarters (Ken Keisatsu Honbu). Tokyo residents apply at the Keishicho (Tokyo Metropolitan Police Department).

How to Get It

  1. Request a letter from the U.S. Embassy confirming you need a police certificate for visa purposes. This letter is required by the Japanese police.
  2. Visit your Prefectural Police Headquarters with the embassy letter, your passport, and Resident Card (Zairyu Card) or Juminhyo.
  3. Processing time: Approximately 2-3 weeks.
  4. Cost: Free.
  5. The certificate will be provided in a sealed envelope. Do NOT open it. If the seal is broken, the certificate is invalid.
The sealed envelope is critical. Some applicants accidentally open it thinking they need to review the contents. Any broken seal means you must restart the process.

Medical Examination

The medical exam must be performed by a USCIS-designated panel physician in Japan. The embassy will provide a list of authorized physicians.

What is included:

  • Complete physical examination
  • Chest X-ray (TB screening)
  • Blood tests (syphilis, HIV)
  • Vaccination review

Cost: approximately $200-$400 (JPY 30,000-60,000). Bring vaccination records from your physician to reduce costs. Results come in a sealed envelope.

Interview Tips for Tokyo

  • Do not open the sealed police certificate. This is the most common Japan-specific mistake.
  • Bring your valid passport (no photocopies accepted) and either Juminhyo or government document showing name and address.
  • Relationship evidence. Photos, travel records, communication logs, and evidence of visits between the U.S. and Japan.
  • Military applicants: If the petitioner is U.S. military in Japan, bring military ID, orders, and evidence that you met in Japan through legitimate means.
  • Know your petitioner's details. Address, employer, salary, daily routine, family members.

Common Issues and Denial Reasons

  • Broken seal on police certificate. Must be re-obtained if opened.
  • Translation quality. Japanese-to-English translations must be certified and accurate. Japanese names in particular must be romanized consistently across all documents.
  • Insufficient relationship evidence. Especially important if there is a language barrier between partners.
  • Financial insufficiency. See our I-134 guide.

After the Visa: What Happens Next

  1. Travel to the U.S. within 6 months.
  2. Marry within 90 days. See our 90-day rule guide.
  3. File Adjustment of Status (I-485, $1,440).
  4. Apply for work authorization (EAD) concurrently.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take from Japan?

Approximately 13-17 months total.

Where is the interview?

U.S. Embassy in Tokyo.

What is the sealed police certificate?

Obtained from your Prefectural Police Headquarters. It must remain sealed — if opened, it is invalid.

What is a Koseki Tohon?

Japan's Family Register extract. It serves as both birth certificate and proof of marital status. Obtained from your municipal office for JPY 450.

Do I need to translate Japanese documents?

Yes. All Japanese documents require certified English translations.

What if the U.S. petitioner is military stationed in Japan?

Military petitioners can file the I-129F from Japan through their base legal office. Bring military ID and orders to the interview.
